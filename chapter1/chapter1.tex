In this chapter, we lay the foundations for the rest of the thesis by carefully introducing the background on which our work builds. We begin by discussing the fundamental notions of \textit{information needs} and \textit{information objects}~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018}. We then turn to the broader themes of information extraction and access, situating the discussion within several relevant branches of computer science---including \acl{nlp}, \acl{ir}, \acl{sw}, and \acl{db}. Once this broader context is established, we narrdescribe the specific knowledge-intensive domains that motivate our study, namely legal judgments and data from criminal investigations. Finally, we review the literature on the concrete tasks that are most directly related to our research---such as \acl{er}, \acl{el}, \acl{cr}, and \acl{qa}.

\section{Information Access}
On a daily basis, we use our personal computer for accessing information. Search engines (Google, Bing, Yahoo!, DuckDuckGo, etc.) and web browsers (Mozilla Firefox, Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, etc.) help us to fullfill our \textit{information needs} by proving the relevant \textit{information objects}. The former aim to find web pages that contain our information need, often represented as a \textit{query}, the latter allow us to browse web pages to finally find the required information.
We will refer to the users' capability of accessing the required information as \textit{\acl{ia}} to generalize over \acl{ir} and search engines by including also technologies that does not retrieve information but still allows users to access the needed information; consider web browsers as an example.
