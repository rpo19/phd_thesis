\subsection{Named Entity Linking}
\begin{definition}
    [Named Entity Linking (NEL)]
    \Acf{nel} is the task of disambiguating and linking entity mentions detected in text to entries in a \acf{kr}, such as Wikipedia, or to entities in a \ac{kb}, such as Wikidata~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog,jr3}.
\end{definition}


While \ac{ner} identifies spans of text that correspond to named entities (e.g., ``Barack Obama''), \ac{nel} goes one step further by resolving the ambiguity of the mention and associating it with a unique identifier in a \ac{kr} (e.g., the Wikidata entity Barack Obama\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/entity/Q76}} or the English Wikipedia page Barack Obama\footnote{\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barack_Obama}}). This process is crucial when mentions can correspond to multiple entities or when different surface forms all refer to the same entity. For example, in the first case, the mention ``Lisbon'' may refer to Lisbon, Capital of Portugal\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/entity/Q597}} or to the movie Lisbon (1956 film)\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/entity/Q3203631}}. Furthermore, ambiguity can arise also for entities with the same type, such as Lisbon, Connecticut\footnote{\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisbon,_Connecticut}}, or the other cities named ``Lisbon''\footnote{\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisbon_(disambiguation)}}.
In the second case, the mentions ``Barack Obama'' and ``President Obama'' should both be linked to the entity Barack Obama, Former US President\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/entity/Q76}}.

Similarly to \ac{ner}, it is part of \ac{ie}, contributing to \acl{sta}, \acl{kbp}, and downstream tasks like entity-based semantic search. In \ac{sta}, besides highlighting entities identified with \ac{ner}, linking them to a \ac{kr} allows to create clickable hyperlinks that let users browse directly to entity pages (e.g., Wikipedia or Wikidata).
Furthermore, in entity-based search applications, \ac{nel} enables more powerful facets and filters based on the entries in the \ac{kb} instead of on the mention surface forms.

Other applications of \ac{nel} are towards \acl{qa} and \acl{ir}. Some \ac{qa} systems rely on entity linking to understand the entities mentioned in the question and retrieve relevant information from a \ac{kr} or a \ac{kg}~\cite{li-etal-2020-efficient,el_differences_for_qa_2022,eal_el_re_for_qa_2018}. Similarly, in \ac{ir}, linked entities support \emph{query understanding}~\cite{el_in_queries_2015}. In some cases, entity-based queries can be fully reduced to a \ac{nel} task, while in others, \ac{nel} serves as a bridge to access the relevant entity facts, as illustrated in \Cref{fig:rome-search,fig:barack-search}.

Formally, given a set of entity mentions $M = \{m_1, m_2, \ldots\}$---for instance, detected by a \ac{ner} system---and a \acl{kb} $KB = \{e_1, e_2, \ldots\}$ containing entities with unique identifiers and descriptions, a \ac{nel} system produces a mapping:
\begin{equation}
f: M \to KB \cup \{\texttt{NIL}\},
\end{equation}
where \texttt{NIL} denotes that the mention does not correspond to any entry in the knowledge base.  
For example, given the sentence ``Barack Obama was born in Hawaii.'' and the mention ``Barack Obama'', the system should map it to the entity \texttt{Q76} (\texttt{Barack Obama}) in Wikidata.

Traditionally, \ac{nel} approaches have been decomposed into two main steps~\cite{shen2015entitylinking}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Candidate Generation}: retrieving a set of possible entities from the \ac{kb} for a given mention, often based on lexical similarity or alias tables.
    \item \textbf{Candidate Ranking}: selecting the most likely entity from the candidates, based on local context, global coherence, and entity popularity.
\end{enumerate}

Early \ac{nel} systems relied heavily on manually curated lexicons, surface-form matching, and heuristics. Later approaches incorporated machine learning models with features such as context similarity, entity popularity, and coherence across mentions~\cite{ratinov2011localglobal}.  
With deep learning, embeddings of mentions and entities in a shared vector space enabled more robust disambiguation~\cite{yamada2016joint,kolitsas-etal-2018-end2end}. State-of-the-art models leverage transformer-based encoders (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) to jointly model mention context and candidate entity descriptions~\cite{wu2020scibertnel,logeswaran2019zero}.

Recent works explore the use of \acp{llm} for \ac{nel}, where entity linking is framed as text generation or retrieval-augmented tasks~\cite{shen2023large,poerner2020bertentitylinking}. For example, prompting an \ac{llm} to output a \texttt{Wikidata ID} or a canonical name, or integrating \ac{kb} lookups through retrieval-augmented generation. These approaches show promising results, especially for zero-shot or low-resource domains, but share the challenges of high computational costs and prompt sensitivity already discussed for \ac{ner}.

Finally, several libraries support \ac{nel}, including DBpedia Spotlight~\cite{mendes2011dbpedia}, BLINK~\cite{wu2020blink}, REL~\cite{van2020rel}, and spaCy’s \texttt{EntityLinker} component~\cite{honnibal2020spacy}. These frameworks provide ready-to-use linking pipelines that combine candidate generation and ranking, and can be fine-tuned for domain-specific applications.
