\chapter{Data Integration Architecture for Knowledge-Intensive Domains}
\label{chap:architecture}

In this chapter we address \ref{ob:architecture} by describing the entity-centric architectural model for integrating heterogeneous data in knowledge-intensive domains, which special focus on the legal domain.

In the past years I have collaborated in several projects, whose aim was to integrate \ac{ai} advancements in the Italian legal domain. As evident by the document types presented in this thesis, my focus has been towards Italian civil judgments and criminal investigations. This started in 2021, when I began collaborating with my future \phd supervisor, Professor Matteo Palmonari, on the application of \acl{ee} techniques on investigative data.
%
This allowed me to acquire experience on this domain and receive feedback from legal professionals about our proposed prototypes and proof-of-concepts.

To remind the reader about the architectural requirements we anticipated in \Cref{sec:intro}, I additionally report them in the following lines. The main principles, that corresponds to non-functional architectural requirements are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generalizability}, to handle heterogeneous documents and adapt to new investigative use cases.  
    \item \textbf{Traceability and Verifiability}, to ensure that every extracted element can be traced back to its textual origin.  
    \item \textbf{Error correction capability}, to enable users to detect and fix extraction or linking errors through human-in-the-loop validation.  
    \item \textbf{Scalability}, to efficiently manage large and continuously growing document collections.  
\end{enumerate}

In addition, we define functional requirements for supporting the domain-specific use cases considered in this thesis---namely (i) case retrieval and precedent search, (ii) document navigation and content exploration, (iii) investigative retrieval, (iv) question answering over documents and collections, and (v) statistical analysis and monitoring.
These functional requirements are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item support for \acs{crud}~\cite{martin1983managing} operation to \acl{crud} documents, entities, and annotations---which store at least the position of a mentions in the document and the link to the entities to which it refers to. These operations are necessary to injest documents in the architecture and also to allow \acp{ui}, such as a document explorer, to acquire and show documents to the users.
    \item Retrieval \acp{api}, supporting both sparse \ac{ir}---for standard keyword-based retrieval or faceted search (i and iii)~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted} and dense semantic retrieval~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}---for question answering (iv). If fact, users may benefit from both: sparse retrieval systems allow to exactly seach for a certain keyword, while \ac{rag} applications generally use dense retrieval~\cite{old_ir_methods_rag_2024}.
    \item A query language and engine to run complex queries that allow to elaborate advanced statistics (v).
\end{enumerate}
 
As anticipated, a core concept on which we design this architecture is the \emph{entity}---a concept so ubiquitous that can integrate highly heterogeneous data, supporting the required generalizability.
Another core concept is the \emph{anontation}, which \say{connect} the enities with the \emph{documents} they have been mentioned in---addressing traceability and verifiability.
The \emph{document--annotation--entity} triad represents, indeed, a corenerstone for our architecture.

The architecture presented in this chapter builds on all these proposed prototypes, with their successes, and their issues. 
Some architectural contributons have alredy been published~\cite{BELLANDI2024,pozzi_wise_2025}---together with the \acl{ee} contributions presented in the previuos chapter (\Cref{chap:extraction}). In \textcite{BELLANDI2024}, one of the two research questions addressed was directed to the architectural design for fulfilling the needs of legal users working with civil judgments. In that work, we defined functional and non-functional requirements, such as support for intra-document and inter-document search for the functionals, and scalability, extensibility, and availability for the non functional~\cite{BELLANDI2024}. Then, we provided an implementation of the architecture and we demostrated it fulfilled the requirements. That architecture predisposed back end and front end components, and was already based on the concepts of \emph{document--annotation--entity}---as highlighted by ``entity-centric'' in the title.
