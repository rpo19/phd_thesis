\chapter[Data Integration Architecture for Knowledge-Intensive Domains][Data Integration Architecture]{Data Integration Architecture for Knowledge-Intensive Domains}
\label{chap:architecture}

In this chapter we address \ref{ob:architecture} by presenting an entity-centric architectural model for integrating heterogeneous data in knowledge-intensive domains, with a particular focus on the legal domain.
Although the work takes inspiration from different projects in the legal domain, the goal is not to reproduce those designs. Rather, we distill a general architecture that can be adopted beyond those specific cases, including other knowledge-intensive domains where entities play a central role in shaping information needs, user interactions, and downstream tasks.



Over the past years I have contributed to several research projects aimed at incorporating \acf{ai} technologies into the Italian legal ecosystem. As shown by the document types considered in this thesis, my work has primarily concentrated on two contexts: Italian civil judgments and criminal investigations. This activity began in 2021, when I started collaborating with my future \phd supervisor, Professor Matteo Palmonari, on the application of \acl{ee} methods to investigative data.
%
During this collaboration I contributed to the development of proofs of concept and architectural prototypes for integrating and managing documents in these contexts, which gave me continuous exposure to real use cases and the opportunity to collect feedback from legal professionals.



In the remainder of this chapter we define the requirements for the architectural design (\Cref{sec:arch:reqs}), then we describe our proposal, including the data model with the chosen interchange format (\Cref{sec:arch:datamodel}) and the model of the architecture (\Cref{sec:arch:mainmodel}), compare it with the prototypes previously developed as part of this \phd{} (\Cref{sec:arch_proto}), and finally describe the advanced \acfp{ui} that can be paired with an implementation of the proposed \acf{dia} (\Cref{sec:iaui-from-prototypes}). The chapter concludes with a summary of the main points (\Cref{sec:arch:conclusion}).

\section{Requirements}
\label{sec:arch:reqs}

As anticipated in \Cref{sec:intro}, the proposed \acl{dia} serves as the bridge between the information extraction and access layers. It ensures that extracted information is stored, integrated, and made accessible in a consistent and verifiable manner.

The architecture is designed to support both structured legal documents (e.g., judgments, legislative acts) and the heterogeneous data produced in investigative contexts. Its design follows a set of guiding principles:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Generalizability} to heterogeneous documents and new use cases, as different investigations may require ad-hoc workflows or specialized processing chains.
  \item \textbf{Traceability and verifiability}, ensuring that the provenance of any extracted information can always be inspected. For example, a user querying for documents mentioning a person should be able to view the original mention in its textual context, while a \acl{qa} component should expose the passages supporting its answers.
  \item \textbf{Error correction capability}, enabling users to identify and correct extraction errors through a \acf{hitl}~\cite{klie-etal-2020-zero} validation approach, supporting human oversight~\cite[Art.~14]{ai_act} and reinforcing system quality.
  \item \textbf{Scalability} to large and continuously growing document collections.
  \item \textbf{Loose coupling} between components, including external services built on top of the architecture, allowing independent evolution, replacement, or reconfiguration of modules (e.g., switching to an alternative search engine or updating external \acl{ner} or \acl{qa} components) without affecting the rest of the system. Loose coupling also contributes to the architecture availability, as failures or maintenance operations affecting a single component do not compromise the operation of the overall architecture.
  \item \textbf{Interoperability} with internal and external systems, to support complex workflows in which several extractors are executed in sequence. For this reason, it is necessary that all services \emph{communicate} using a \acf{uniof}, so that sequential workflows can be seamlessly modified by adding or removing extractors.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Document--Annotation--Entity Triad}
Before introducing the functional requirements that support the architectural principles and the domain-specific use cases, we first define the three conceptual pillars that underpin our representation of heterogeneous textual data: \emph{documents}, \emph{annotations}, and \emph{entities}.

A \emph{document} is the abstract unit of textual data in our model. Its granularity is not fixed and may range from entire legal judgments to individual chat messages, depending on the use case. Documents serve as the primary carriers of text to which structured information can be attached.

An \emph{entity}, defined in \Cref{back:entities}, represents conceptual objects of interest that can be mentioned across documents. Entities enable the integration of heterogeneous sources into a unified knowledge representation, supporting the generalizability requirement.

An \emph{annotation} establishes a structured link between a document and a portion of its content, optionally associating it with an entity. Annotations serve as the mechanism that enables traceability from structured representations back to their textual evidence (traceability and verifiability), and they generalize beyond entity mentions to any meaningful fragment of text. For example they can represent document sections, or events.

\noindent
We now introduce the additional functional requirements that support the domain-specific use cases considered in this thesis.
\Cref{tab:requirements} summarizes both the principal architectural requirements and the additional functional requirements, together with the use cases they support.
For convenience, and to remind the reader of the domain-specific use cases defined in \Cref{sec:intro}, we briefly restate them here:
\begin{itemize}
  \item case retrieval and precedent search (\ref{uc:case-retrieval});
  \item document navigation and content exploration (\ref{uc:navigation});
  \item investigative retrieval (\ref{uc:investigative-retrieval});
  \item question answering over documents and collections (\ref{uc:qa});
  \item statistical analysis and performance monitoring (\ref{uc:stats}).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
The additional functional requirements are the following:
\begin{itemize}
  \renewcommand\labelitemi{$\diamond$}
    \item \emph{\acf{crud}~\cite{martin1983managing} \acp{api}} for creating, reading, updating, deleting documents, entities, and annotations. However, an important exception need to be applied: since the annotations refer and depend on the document text, modifying it should normally be forbidden as it invalidates all the existing annotations.
    \item Support for \emph{multiple collections of documents}. While, for example, storing data from different investigations in the same \ac{dia} instance may not be advisable due to risks in data protection, this requirement allows handling---and interlinking via \acp{uri}---different but related set of documents, or to host multiple \say{models} of the same data. As a practical example derived from the experience as investigative consultant, investigator may need to visualize entire chats from \acf{ima} data and also to navigate single messages---\eg searching for messages sent in a specific time interval. In the first scenario it is preferable to model chats as documents, while in the second to atomically model messages as documents. This requirement enables this kind of flexibility, also allowing to interlink items, \eg for visualizing the entire chat containing a message. Finally, different document collections should be allowed to refer to different \aclp{kr}.
    \item Support for \emph{annotation versioning}. The architectural model prototyped in previous work~\cite{BELLANDI2024} was already able to manage multiple versions of the same document. This functionality was introduced for storing the original document and additional pre-processed copies. In this formalization of the \ac{dia} we simplify this functionality to versioning only the annotations---which can be modified by humans through the Error correction \ac{ui}---in order to allow \emph{error correction capability} and \emph{traceability} at the same time. For fully supporting these principles, we borrow consolidated concepts from version control systems for software development~\cite{version_control_survey_2018}: it should be possible to maintain, and potentially restore, previous versions of all the annotations and any modification should be recorded with an identifier of the user who performed the operation and an explanatory message describing the changes---similarly to a git commit~\cite{version_control_survey_2018}. For more complex needs requiring documents' modifications, users can rely on the support for \emph{multiple collections} and ingest the processed documents in a new collection.
    \item \textit{Retrieval \acp{api}}, supporting:
      \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item sparse \acf{ir} for keyword-based retrieval or faceted search
              (\ref{uc:case-retrieval}, \ref{uc:investigative-retrieval})~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted};
        \item dense semantic retrieval for question answering~(\ref{uc:qa})~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021};
        \item hybrid retrieval combining sparse and dense~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}.
      \end{itemize}
      In fact, users may benefit from both: sparse retrieval systems allow to exactly search for a certain keyword; while \ac{rag} applications generally use dense retrieval~\cite{old_ir_methods_rag_2024}; and combining both may provide complementary information~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}.
    \item Support for a \emph{\acf{ql}} capable of expressing complex queries to enable advanced statistical analysis~(\ref{uc:stats}).
    \item \emph{Visualization \ac{ui}} that allow users to visualize documents with automatic annotations, to trace and verify the mentioned entities, in support of the requirement for \emph{traceability and verifiability}.
    \item \emph{Error correction \ac{ui}} that allow users to access documents and automatic annotations, to correct machine errors in support of the requirement for \emph{error correction capability}.
\end{itemize}
Moreover, to support users in pursuing their \acl{ia} needs for the considered use cases, we propose several \acp{ui} in \Cref{sec:iaui-from-prototypes}, while in \Cref{sec:arch_proto} we discuss the differences between our proposal for a \acf{dia} and the previous works~\cite{BELLANDI2024,pozzi_wise_2025,Agazzi2025DAVE} that have contributed to the expertise underlying the formalization presented in this chapter.

However, differently from those works, the present chapter---which addresses \ref{ob:architecture}---focuses strictly on the \acl{dia}. Some design principles for peripheral services (\eg extractors) and \acp{ui} are discussed, but they are considered external to the \ac{dia} core. Our objective is to define the \emph{minimum necessary} directives to guide the implementation of a \acl{dia} for knowledge-intensive domains---and, in particular, for the legal domain---so that it provides the fundamental functionality required to satisfy the architectural principles.

\label{sec:arch:datamodel}

As anticipated, to support the integration of heterogeneous data sources within a unified representation, we formalize three core concepts: \emph{documents}, \emph{annotations}, and \emph{entities}. These concepts establish the structural basis for storing and integrating heterogeneous data from knowledge-intensive domains. Their design is inspired by the GateNLP~\cite{cunningham-etal-2002-gate,gatenlp} project, from which we adopt the notions of documents\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/document.html}}, annotations\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/annotation.html}}, and annotation sets\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/annotation_set.html}}---that allows to organize annotations in sets---as well as the use of key--value metadata.

GateNLP~\cite{cunningham-etal-2002-gate,gatenlp} is a Python framework for natural language processing that provides a flexible representation of documents and annotations, also offering interactive visualization in Jupyter notebooks.

We also adopt GateNLP python objects serialized in JSON format~\cite{rfc8259} as the \emph{data interchange format}, to achieve interoperability of different services. The integration of dedicated services, indeed, is supported by the designed \ac{dia} for the purpose of generalizability, as dedicated processors may be required by specific use case.
For example, the input of the \emph{create document} \ac{api} or the output of systems that produce annotations, such as for \acl{ner} and \acl{nel}, must be in \emph{GateJSON} format---which means an instance of the GateNLP document object\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/document.html}} serialized in JSON. The serialized documents, indeed, also contain the annotation sets and the annotations, as shown in \Cref{lst:gatejson-format}.
This choice also maintain compatibility with GateNLP functions (after deserializing documents into Python objects) which support advanced operations on annotation sets, useful, for instance, to identify overlapping annotations, or annotations contained in another annotation.

As a real example, suppose to have a document, derived from a legal judgment, with two annotation sets, one containing annotations for the sections of the judgment, and the second containing \ac{ner} annotation for the people involved in the judgment. For identifying plaintiffs and defendants we usually focus on the beginning of the document, in the section ``identification of the parties''. So given this section, with GateNLP \acp{api} we are able to obtain which people are mentioned in that section.

To clearly define the components of our document--annotation--entity triad, additionally enriched by annotation sets, we describe each element separately and provide a conceptual schema in \Cref{fig:data-model}.
%in the following enumeration. This structured presentation highlights the properties and relationships of documents, annotations, and annotation sets, and clarifies their role in supporting traceability, interoperability, and advanced annotation-based operations.



\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Document.}  
  A document represents a general unit of data and may vary in granularity depending on the use case---for instance, a legal judgment, a contract, or a single \acs{ima} message.  
  Each document is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri}, ensuring global identifiability;
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item the \emph{text content};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.  
  \end{itemize}
  Documents serve as the source layer for annotations and the reference point for traceability.

  \item \textbf{Annotation.}  
  An annotation connects a portion of a document to a specific concept or entity, thereby linking unstructured text with structured knowledge. An annotation is defined by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{source document}, ensuring that the annotation can always be traced back to its origin---even when processed within collections derived from another collection;
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{annotation set} it is part of, for ensuring traceability;
    \item the \emph{start} and \emph{end} character offsets in the document text;
    \item the \emph{type} of the annotation, \eg the \ac{ner} type, or the name of a section;
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, as key--value pairs, representing additional properties, \eg the linked entity \ac{uri} for \ac{nel} annotation or the version of the annotation algorithm.  
  \end{itemize}
  Annotations allow users to visualize, verify, and trace where entities are mentioned in documents. Beyond entity linking, this concept generalizes to any document fragment, from short spans of text to entire sections, enabling flexible annotation schemas.

  \item \textbf{Annotation Set.}  
  An annotation set is a collection of annotations organized according to a shared criterion, such as annotation type, purpose, or version. This structure allows for a better organization of the annotations and for the coexistence of multiple annotation layers over the same document (\eg manual vs.\ automatic annotations, or distinct semantic layers), as well as for annotation versioning---which can be achieved by creating a modified annotation set, saving the version number, the user who modified it, and a message explaining the changes in the annotation set's metadata.  
  Each annotation set includes:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{document} to which the annotation set refers;
    \item a \emph{name}, describing the set (\eg ``\ac{ner}-v1'' or ``\ac{nel}-v1'');
    \item a list of contained \emph{annotations};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}
%   Managing annotations through sets facilitates versioning, organization by task or algorithm, and selective querying.
  \item \textbf{Entity.}  
    Each entity, in line with the definition in \Cref{back:entities} is characterized by:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item a unique \ac{uri}, ensuring global identifiability;
        \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
        \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs. For example, metadata may contain the entity type (or types).
    \end{itemize}
    % Entities provide a generalizable way to interlink heterogeneous documents and annotations.

  \item \textbf{Document Collection.}
  A document collection contains multiple documents and provides a way to organize and manage different documents separately in the same \ac{dia} instance.
  Each document collection is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{knowledge repository} considered by this collection;
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item a list of contained \emph{documents};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Knowledge Repository.}
  We additionally include the \ac{kr} in the data model to enable using different principal \acp{kr} with different document collections. Indeed, while \acp{uri} already allow referring to multiple \acp{kr}, including public ones, a principal \ac{kr} is needed for representing new entities (see \nilchallenge in \Cref{sec:intro}) and using only one \ac{kr} for different document collections may generate conflicts.
  In \Cref{fig:data-model}, \acf{krms} refers to a system that manages multiple \acp{kr}, similarly to \acfp{dbms}, which can hold multiple \aclp{db}.
  A \ac{kr} is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item a list of contained \emph{entities};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}

\end{enumerate}

While GateNLP uses internal numeric identifiers for its objects, we extend it by introducing globally unique \acp{uri}, which simplify locating resources, enabling inter-document linking, and the use with distributed storage.
Furthermore, \acp{uri} allow for the seamless integration of public \acp{kr} and \acp{kb}, allowing to link annotations to entities from Wikipedia, Wikidata, or domain specific \acp{kr}.

In \Cref{fig:data-model}, entities are placed in the \acl{kr}, since they are stored and managed there, as described in the next section.




\section{Architectural Model}
\label{sec:arch:mainmodel}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/architectural_model.drawio.pdf}
  \caption{High-level architectural model of the \acl{dia}.}
  \label{fig:architectural-model}
\end{figure}

An overview of the \acl{dia} is depicted in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}. The figure shows a clear division between what is part of the \ac{dia} and what is outside it.
Inside we find the \emph{storage layer}, whose objective is to store information regarding documents, annotations, and entities, as well as to organize this information for efficient access via indexing.
This layer is composed of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{\Acf{ad}}, which stores documents, annotations, and annotation sets as defined in the data model, supporting multiple document collections.
  \item \emph{\Acf{krms}}, which stores local entity knowledge, eventually in multiple \aclp{kr} (see \Cref{back:entities} for the definitions of entity and \acl{kr}). Note that, while in the \ac{dia} design this is not enforced, the \ac{kr} components can be \aclp{kb} holding facts about entities in the form of \acs{rdf} triples.
  \item \emph{\Acf{ri}}, which holds indexes for efficient retrieval and supports sparse, dense, and hybrid retrieval paradigms.
\end{itemize}

On top of the storage layer sits the \ac{api} layer, which exposes interfaces to interact with the persisted data. This is the component that must provide the mandatory \acp{api} defined by the requirements:
\begin{itemize}
  \renewcommand\labelitemi{$\diamond$}
  \item \emph{\ac{crud} \acp{api}} for all the resources in the data model: documents, annotations, annotation sets, and document collections, stored in the \ac{ad}, entities and \aclp{kr}, stored in the \ac{krms}.
  \item \emph{Sparse, dense, and hybrid retrieval \acp{api}} using the indexes from the \ac{ri} for efficient retrieval.
  \item \emph{\acl{ql} \acp{api}} supporting aggregation and filtering operations required to compute statistics, derive distributions, and answer structured analytical queries over documents, annotations, and entities. These interfaces must support at least the standard family of operations found in declarative query languages---such as grouping and aggregation (\eg counts, distinct counts, maxima/minima, or averages)---as documented in mainstream \acp{dbms} (\eg PostgreSQL~\cite{postgresql-agg-doc}). Such capabilities are essential to support use case~\ref{uc:stats}, \ie statistical analysis and monitoring. This requirement is reflected in the implementation choice of the annotation \ac{db}, which has to support such operations.
\end{itemize}
These \acp{api} must operate over the network to support distributed deployments, enabling parallel processing and horizontal scalability. Furthermore, they must support the GateJSON format for both input and output of annotated documents, ensuring interoperability across heterogeneous components.

Besides the storage and \ac{api} layers, the \ac{dia} features the two fundamental \acp{ui} for fulfilling traceability-verifiability and error-correction capability.
The \emph{Visualization \ac{ui}} must provide the following functionalities to users:
\begin{itemize}
  \item visualize annotated documents, selecting which annotation sets to visualize;
  \item access the properties (defined in the data model) of documents, annotation sets, annotations, and entities, while visualizing the annotated document---\eg by clicking on an annotation to inspect annotation properties---or given the \ac{uri} of the resource;
  \item given an entity, trace which annotations mentioned it and visualize them in the document.
\end{itemize}

On the other hand, the \emph{Error correction \ac{ui}} provides functionalities to correct annotation errors from automatic tools and, in general, provides delete operations for removing automatically created resources whose creation was an error---such as duplicated entities in the \ac{kr}. While additional functionalities, such as manual ingesting of documents, may be useful, we only include the minimum necessary design principles for allowing \emph{error correction capability}.
\begin{itemize}
  % \item For documents:
  % \begin{itemize}
  %   \item create from name, text, and metadata, assigning a URI; % not error correction...
  % \end{itemize}
  \item For documents, it only provides the delete operation, which consequently deletes all the associated annotation sets and annotations.
  \item Also, for document collections it provides the delete operation, which consequently deletes all documents and annotations.
  \item Similarly, for \aclp{kr}, it allows for deletion, which propagates to all contained entities.
  \item For annotations, it provides the following operations:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item create;
    \item delete;
    \item move---same as modify start and end;
    \item modify properties (type and metadata)---for example to change the linked entity;
    \item versioning: each modification---with annexed the user ID and a message explaining the changes---must be persisted in a \emph{history} at the annotation set level. This centralizes the annotation versioning management to the annotation set, allowing to visualize the history of the entire annotation set and to perform rollback operations. 
  \end{itemize}
  \item For annotation sets, it allows to:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item delete a set, together with all the annotations;
    \item view history, including user IDs and message for each modification;
    \item rollback to a specific version, undoing all the modifications performed afterwards.
  \end{itemize}
  \item For entities, it allows to:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item create a new entity to manually assign it to annotations;
    \item merge two entities and consequently assign all the linked annotations to the new entity;
    \item delete an entity and optionally chose another entity to assign to the linked annotations and get the list of affected annotations for manually revising them;
    \item modify name and metadata.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

We conclude the core architectural description by defining three information flows that the \ac{dia} must support. These flows cover all interactions with external services---such as preprocessing components, text annotators, or \acp{ui}---and assume interoperability through the GateJSON format. We also recall the design guideline of ensuring loose coupling among services to reduce cascading failures.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{\Acl{ie} flow.}
External services process raw data, serialize it in GateJSON, and inject it into the \ac{dia} via \acp{api}. This corresponds to the green arrows in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}, incoming from outside to the core of the \ac{dia}.

\item \emph{\Acl{ia} flow.}
\acp{ui} retrieve information stored in the \ac{dia} via read or retrieval \acp{api} (using GateJSON) and present it to users. This corresponds to the outgoing red arrow in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}.

\item \emph{Circular flow.}
Some services both read from and write to the \ac{dia}, such as sequential processors that enrich documents (e.g.\ by adding annotation sets). The \emph{Error-correction \ac{ui}} is similar: it reads from the \ac{dia} and persists human-made revisions back into it.
\end{enumerate}

These three flows enable external services to ingest, access, and iteratively refine information, allowing the \ac{dia} to generalize across use cases.
Moreover, executing processing steps on top of previously generated results, combined with network-based \acp{api}, enables complex workflows and distributed execution.
The ability to operate on separate annotation sets minimizes conflicts among concurrent processes, while loose coupling supports scalability when combined with an orchestrator. The systematic use of \acp{uri} ensures that documents and annotations can be retrieved unambiguously via the \acp{api}.
