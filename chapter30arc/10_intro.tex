\chapter{Data Integration Architecture for Knowledge-Intensive Domains}
\label{chap:architecture}

In this chapter we address \ref{ob:architecture} by presenting an entity-centric architectural model for integrating heterogeneous data in knowledge-intensive domains, with a particular focus on the legal domain.

Over the past years I have contributed to several research projects aimed at incorporating \ac{ai} technologies into the Italian legal ecosystem. As shown by the document types considered in this thesis, my work has primarily concentrated on two contexts: Italian civil judgments and criminal investigations. This activity began in 2021, when I started collaborating with my future \phd supervisor, Professor Matteo Palmonari, on the application of \acl{ee} methods to investigative data.
%
This collaboration provided continuous exposure to real use cases and the opportunity to collect feedback from legal professionals on our prototypes and proof-of-concepts.

From these projects, three works have been published (all co-authored by me), in chronological order:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textcite{BELLANDI2024}, which presents an entity-centric infrastructure for managing legal documents, including architectural components, an \ac{nlp} pipeline for \ac{ee} from civil court judgments to annotate documents, and \acl{ia} \acp{ui}, such as entity-based document exploration, and a search interface.     \item \textbf{Traceability and Verifiability}, to ensure that every extracted element can be traced back to its textual origin.\todo{annotations}  
    \item \textbf{Error correction capability}, to enable users to detect and fix extraction or linking errors through human-in-the-loop validation.\todo{this requires a UI!}
    \item \textbf{Scalability}, to efficiently manage large and continuously growing document collections.  \todo{processing orchestration? parallelizable operations, queues,... strong scalable database systems}

We also borrow the \textbf{availability} requirement from previous work~\cite{BELLANDI2024}, which aims at ensuring that the \ac{dia} does not suffer from changes or failures in other services such as extractors---\ie \acl{ee} tools that annotate documents with entities---or \aclp{ui}. Moreover, this requirement extends to the ecosystem of services operating around the \ac{dia}. For example, extraction tools and all other services built around the \ac{dia} should be designed for
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Loose coupling}, to minimize cross-dependencies and prevent failures or changes in one service from propagating to others;
    \item \textbf{Interoperability}, to support complex workflows in which several extractors are executed in sequence. For this reason, it is necessary that all services \emph{communicate} using a \ac{uniof}, so that sequential workflows can be seamlessly modified by adding or removing extractors.
\end{itemize}
Loose coupling also indirectly improves scalability since independent services can run in parallel.

\paragraph{Document--Annotation--Entity Triad}
Before introducing the functional requirements that support the architectural principles and the domain-specific use cases, we first define the three conceptual pillars that underpin our representation of heterogeneous textual data: \emph{documents}, \emph{annotations}, and \emph{entities}.

A \emph{document} is the abstract unit of textual data in our model. Its granularity is not fixed and may range from entire legal judgments to individual chat messages, depending on the use case. Documents serve as the primary carriers of text to which structured information can be attached.

An \emph{entity}, defined in \Cref{back:entities}, represents conceptual objects of interest that can be mentioned across documents. Entities enable the integration of heterogeneous sources into a unified knowledge representation, supporting the generalizability requirement.

An \emph{annotation} establishes a structured link between a document and a portion of its content, optionally associating it with an entity. Annotations serve as the mechanism that enables traceability from structured representations back to their textual evidence (traceability and verifiability), and they generalize beyond entity mentions to any meaningful fragment of text. For example they can represent document sections, or events.

We now introduce the additional functional requirements that support the domain-specific use cases considered in this thesis. As described in \Cref{sec:intro}, these use cases are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item case retrieval and precedent search (\ref{uc:case-retrieval});
  \item document navigation and content exploration (\ref{uc:navigation});
  \item investigative retrieval (\ref{uc:investigative-retrieval});
  \item question answering over documents and collections (\ref{uc:qa});
  \item statistical analysis and performance monitoring (\ref{uc:stats}).
\end{enumerate}

The additional functional requirements are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{\acf{crud}~\cite{martin1983managing} \acp{api}} for creating, reading, updating, deleting documents, entities, and annotations. However, an important exceptions need to be applied: since the annotations refer and depend on the document text, modifying it should normally be forbidden as it invalidates all the existing annotations. \todo{what happens if i modify a document with annotations?} % uri already required by the data model
    \item Support for \emph{multiple collections of documents}. While, for example, storing data from different investigations in the same \ac{dia} instance may not be advisable due to risks in data protection, this requirement allows to handle different---and interlink via \acp{uri}---different but related set of documents, or to host multiple \say{models} of the same data. As a practical example derived from the investigative consultancy of \textcite{pozzi_wise_2025}, investigator may need to visualize entire chats from \ac{ima} data and also to navigate single messages---\eg searching for messages sent in a specific time interval. In the first scenario it is preferable to model chats as documents, while in the second to atomically model messages as documents. This requirement enables this kind of flexibility, also allowing to interlink items, \eg for visualizing the entire chat containing a message. Finally, different document collections should be allowed to refer to different \aclp{kr}.
    \item Support for \emph{annotation versioning}. Our previous architectural model~\cite{BELLANDI2024} was already able to manage multiple versions of the same document. This functionality was introduced for storing the original document and additional pre-processed copies. In this formalization of the \ac{dia} we simplify this functionality to versioning only the annotations---which can be modified by humans through the Error correction \ac{ui}---in order to allow \emph{error correction capability} and \emph{traceability} at the same time. For fully supporting these principles, we borrow consolidated concepts from version control systems for software development~\cite{version_control_survey_2018}: it should be possible to maintain, and potentially restore, previous versions of all the annotations and any modification should be recorded with an identifier of the user who performed the operation and an explanatory message describing the changes---similarly to a git commit~\cite{version_control_survey_2018}. For more complex needs requiring documents' modifications, users can rely on the support for \emph{multiple collections} and ingest the processed documents in a new collection.
    \item \textit{Retrieval \acp{api}}, supporting both sparse \ac{ir}---for standard keyword-based retrieval or faceted search (\ref{uc:case-retrieval} and \ref{uc:investigative-retrieval})~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted}---dense semantic retrieval~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}---for question answering \ref{uc:qa}---and hybrid retrieval combining both sparse and dense~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}. In fact, users may benefit from both: sparse retrieval systems allow to exactly search for a certain keyword; while \ac{rag} applications generally use dense retrieval~\cite{old_ir_methods_rag_2024}; and combining sparse may provide complementary information~\cite{negar_sparse_dense_retrieval_2021}.
    \item Support for a \emph{\acf{ql}} capable of expressing complex queries to enable advanced statistical analysis \ref{uc:stats}.
    \item \emph{Visualization \ac{ui}} that allow users to visualize documents with automatic annotations, to trace and verify the mentioned entities, in support of the requirement for \emph{traceability and verifiability}.
    \item \emph{Error correction \ac{ui}} that allow users to access documents and automatic annotations, to correct machine errors in support of the requirement for \emph{error correction capability}.
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, to support users in pursuing their \acl{ia} needs for the considered use cases, several \acp{ui} and architectural prototypes have been proposed during my \phd and published in~\cite{BELLANDI2024,pozzi_wise_2025,Agazzi2025DAVE}. These prototypes---described in \Cref{sec:arch_proto,sec:iaui}---and their respective successes and limitations contributed to the expertise underlying the formalization presented in this chapter.

However, differently from those works, the present chapter---which addresses \ref{ob:architecture}---focuses strictly on the \acl{dia}. Some design principles for peripheral services (\eg extractors) and \acp{ui} are discussed, but they are considered external to the \ac{dia} core. Our objective is to define the \emph{minimum necessary} directives to guide the implementation of a \acl{dia} for knowledge-intensive domains---in particular for the legal domain---so that it provides the fundamental functionality required to satisfy the architectural principles.

As anticipated, to support the integration of heterogeneous data sources within a unified representation, we formalize three core concepts: \emph{documents}, \emph{annotations}, and \emph{entities}. These concepts establish the structural basis for storing and integrating heterogeneous data from knowledge-intensive domains. Their design is inspired by the GateNLP~\cite{cunningham-etal-2002-gate,gatenlp} model, as described in its documentation, from which we adopt the notions of documents\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/document.html}}, annotations\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/annotation.html}}, and annotation sets\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/annotation_set.html}}---that allows to organize annotations in sets---as well as the use of key--value metadata, corresponding to the ``features'' property of GateNLP objects.
%
GateNLP~\cite{cunningham-etal-2002-gate,gatenlp} is a Python framework for natural language processing that provides a flexible representation of documents and annotations, also offering interactive visualization in Jupyter notebooks.

We also chose GateNLP python objects serialized in JSON format~\cite{rfc8259} as the \emph{data interchange format}, to achieve interoperability of services. The integration of dedicated services, indeed, is supported by the designed \ac{dia} for the purpose of generalizability, as dedicated processors may be required by specific use case.
For example, the input of the \emph{create document} \ac{api} or the output of systems that produce annotations, such as for \ac{ner} and \ac{nel}, must be in \emph{GateJSON} format---which means an instance of the GateNLP document object\footnote{\url{https://gatenlp.github.io/python-gatenlp/pythondoc/gatenlp/document.html}} serialized in JSON. The serialized documents, indeed, also contain the annotation sets and the annotations.
This choice also maintain compatibility with GateNLP functions---after deserializing documents into Python objects---which support advanced operations on annotation sets, useful, for instance, to identify overlapping annotations, or annotations contained in another annotation. 
As a real example, suppose to have a document, derived from a legal judgment, with two annotation sets, one containing annotations for the sections of the judgment, and the second containing \ac{ner} annotation for the people involved in the judgment. For identifying plaintiffs and defendants we usually focus on the beginning of the document with the section ``identification of the parties''. So given this section, with GateNLP \acp{api} we are able to obtain which people are mentioned in that section:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Example of using GateNLP~\cite{cunningham-etal-2002-gate,gatenlp} \acp{api}.}, label={lst:gatenlp-section-example}]
from gatenlp import Document
import json

# Load the JSON serialized document
with open("judgment_doc.json") as file:
    raw_doc = json.load(file)

# Deserialize the document
doc = Document.from_dict(raw_doc)

# Get the annotation sets
sections = doc.annset("sections")
people = doc.annset("persons")

# Select the section of interest, in this case the first
parties_id_section = sections[0]

# Retrieve all person annotations contained in that section
mentions = people.within(parties_id_section)
\end{lstlisting}

To clearly define the components of our document--annotation--entity triad, additionally enriched by annotation sets, we describe each element separately and provide a conceptual schema in \Cref{fig:data-model}.
%in the following enumeration. This structured presentation highlights the properties and relationships of documents, annotations, and annotation sets, and clarifies their role in supporting traceability, interoperability, and advanced annotation-based operations.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

  \caption{Data model for documents, annotations, annotation sets, and entities.}
  \label{fig:data-model}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Document.}  
  A document represents a general unit of data and may vary in granularity depending on the use case---for instance, a legal judgment, a contract, or a single instant messaging message.  
  Each document is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri}, ensuring global identifiability;
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item the \emph{text content};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs in a feature dictionary.  
  \end{itemize}
  Documents serve as the source layer for annotations and the reference point for traceability.

  \item \textbf{Annotation.}  
  An annotation connects a portion of a document to a specific concept or entity, thereby linking unstructured text with structured knowledge. An annotation is defined by:\todo{add uri of annotation set?}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{source document}, ensuring that the annotation can always be traced back to its origin---even when processed within collections derived from another collection;
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{annotation set} of which the annotation is part, similarly for ensuring traceability;
    \item the \emph{start} and \emph{end} character offsets in the document text;
    \item the \emph{type} of the annotation, \eg the \ac{ner} type, or the name of a section;
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, as key--value pairs, representing additional properties, \eg the linked entity \ac{uri} for \ac{nel} annotation or the version of the annotation algorithm.  
  \end{itemize}
  Annotations allow users to visualize, verify, and trace where entities are mentioned in documents. Beyond entity linking, this concept generalizes to any document fragment, from short spans of text to entire sections, enabling flexible annotation schemas.

  \item \textbf{Annotation Set.}  
  An annotation set is a collection of annotations organized according to a shared criterion, such as annotation type, purpose, or version. This structure allows for a better organization of the annotations and for the coexistence of multiple annotation layers over the same document (\eg manual vs.\ automatic annotations, or distinct semantic layers), as well as for annotation versioning---which can be achieved by creating a modified annotation set, saving the version number, the user who modified it, and a message explaining the changes in the annotation set's metadata.  
  Each annotation set includes:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{document} to which the annotation set refers;
    \item a \emph{name}, describing the set (\eg ``\ac{ner}-v1'' or ``\ac{nel}-v1'');
    \item a list of contained \emph{annotations};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as feature key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}
%   Managing annotations through sets facilitates versioning, organization by task or algorithm, and selective querying.
  \item \textbf{Entity.}  
    Each entity, in line with the definition in \Cref{back:entities} is characterized by:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item a unique \ac{uri}, ensuring global identifiability;
        \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
        \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs in a feature dictionary. For example, metadata may contain the entity type (or types).
    \end{itemize}
    % Entities provide a generalizable way to interlink heterogeneous documents and annotations.

  \item \textbf{Document Collection.}
  A document collection contains multiple documents and provides a way to organize and manage different documents separately in the same \ac{dia} instance.
  Each document collection is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item the \ac{uri} of the \emph{knowledge repository} considered by this collection;
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item a list of contained \emph{documents};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Knowledge Repository.}
  We additionally include the \ac{kr} in the data model to enable using different principal \acp{kr} with different document collections. Indeed, while \acp{uri} already allow to refer to multiple \acp{kr}, including public ones, a principal \ac{kr} is needed for representing new entities (see \nilchallenge in \Cref{sec:intro}) and using only one \ac{kr} for different document collections may generate conflicts.
  In \Cref{fig:data-model}, \acf{krms} refers to a system that manages multiple \acp{kr}, similarly to \acfp{dbms}, which can hold multiple \aclp{db}.
  A \ac{kr} is characterized by:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item a unique \ac{uri};
    \item a \emph{name}, used as a human-readable label;
    \item a list of contained \emph{entities};
    \item optional \emph{metadata}, stored as key--value pairs.
  \end{itemize}

\end{enumerate}

The metadata, present in each model, derive from GateNLP's general-purpose \emph{features} property, implemented as a key--value dictionary. While GateNLP uses internal numeric identifiers for its objects, we extend it by introducing globally unique \acp{uri}, simplify locating resources, enabling inter-document linking, and simplifies the compatibility with distributed storage.
Furthermore, \acp{uri} allow for the seamless integration of public \acp{kr} and \acp{kb}, allowing to link annotations to entities from Wikipedia, Wikidata, or even domain specific \acp{kr}.

In \Cref{fig:data-model}, entities are placed in the \acl{kr}. This is because entities are stored and managed in the \acl{kr}, as we will describe in the next section.




\section{Architectural Model}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/architectural_model.drawio.pdf}
  \caption{High-level architectural model of the \acl{dia}.}
  \label{fig:architectural-model}
\end{figure}

An overview of the \acl{dia} is depicted in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}. The figure shows a clear division between what is part of the \ac{dia} and what is outside it.
Inside we find the \emph{storage layer}, whose objective is to store information regarding documents, annotations, and entities, as well as to organize this information for efficient access via indexing.
This layer is composed of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{\Acf{ad}}, which stores documents, annotations, and annotation sets as defined in the data model.
  \item \emph{\Acf{kr}}, which stores local entity knowledge, according to the definitions of entity and \ac{kr} from \Cref{back:entities}. Note that, while in the \ac{dia} design this is not enforced, the \ac{kr} component can be a \acl{kb} holding facts about entities in the form of \ac{rdf} triples.
  \item \emph{\Acf{ri}}, which holds indexes for efficient retrieval and supports sparse, dense, and hybrid retrieval paradigms.
\end{itemize}

On top of the storage layer sits the \ac{api} layer, which exposes interfaces to interact with the persisted data. This is the component that must provide the mandatory \acp{api} defined by the requirements:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{\ac{crud} \acp{api}} for all the resources in the data model: documents, annotations, annotation sets, and document collections---stored in the \ac{ad}---entities and \aclp{kr}---stored in the \ac{krms}.
  \item \emph{Sparse, dense, and hybrid retrieval \acp{api}} using the indexes from the \ac{ri} for efficient retrieval.
  \item \emph{\acl{ql} \acp{api}} supporting aggregation and filtering operations required to compute statistics, derive distributions, and answer structured analytical queries over documents, annotations, and entities. These interfaces must support at least the standard family of operations found in declarative query languages---such as grouping and aggregation (\eg counts, distinct counts, maxima/minima, or averages)---as documented in mainstream \acp{dbms} (\eg PostgreSQL~\cite{postgresql-agg-doc}). Such capabilities are essential to support use case~\ref{uc:stats}, \ie statistical analysis and monitoring. This requirements reflects in the implementative choice of the annotation \ac{db}\todo{check for plural ac db missing the p in the back or preface}, which has to support such operations.
\end{itemize}
These \acp{api} must operate over the network to support distributed deployments, enabling parallel processing and horizontal scalability. Furthermore, they must support the GateJSON format for both input and output of annotated documents, ensuring interoperability across heterogeneous components.

Besides the storage and \ac{api} layers, the \ac{dia} features the two fundamental \acp{ui} for fulfilling traceability-verifiability and error-correction capability.
The \emph{Visualization \ac{ui}} must provide the following functionalities to users:
\begin{itemize}
  \item visualize annotated documents, selecting which annotation sets to visualize;
  \item access the properties (defined in the data model) of documents, annotation sets, annotations, and entities, while visualizing the annotated document---\eg by clicking on an annotation to inspect annotation properties---or given the \ac{uri} of the resource;
  \item given an entity, trace which annotations mentioned it and visualize them in the document.
\end{itemize}

On the other hand, the \emph{Error correction \ac{ui}} provides functionalities to correct annotation errors from automatic tools and, in general, provides delete operations for removing automatically created resources whose creation was an error---such as duplicated entities in the \ac{kr}. While additional functionalities, such as manual ingesting of documents, may be useful, we only include the minimum necessary design principles for allowing \emph{error correction capability}.
\begin{itemize}
  % \item For documents:
  % \begin{itemize}
  %   \item create from name, text, and metadata, assigning a URI; % not error correction...
  % \end{itemize}
  \item For documents it only provides the delete operation, which consequently deletes all the associated annotation sets and annotations.
  \item Also for document collections it provides the delete operation, which consequently deletes all documents and annotations.
  \item Similarly for knowledge repository it allows for deletion, which propagates to all contained entities.
  \item For annotations:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item create;
    \item delete;
    \item move---same as modify start and end;
    \item modify properties (type and metadata)---for example to changed the linked entity;
    \item for versioning, each modification---with annexed the user id and a message explaining the changes---must be persisted in a \emph{history} at the annotation set level. This centralizes the annotation versioning management to the annotation set, allowing to visualize the history of the entire annotation set and to perform rollback operations. 
  \end{itemize}
  \item For annotation sets:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item delete together with all the annotations;
    \item view history, including user ids and message for each modification;
    \item rollback to a specific version, undoing all the modifications performed afterwards.
  \end{itemize}
  \item For entities it allows to:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item create a new entity to manually assign it to annotations;
    \item merge two entities and consequently assign all the linked annotations to the new entity;
    \item delete an entity and optionally chose another entity to assign to the linked annotations and get the list of affected annotations for manually revising them;
    \item modify name and metadata.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

We conclude the core architectural description by defining three information flows that the \ac{dia} must support. These flows cover all interactions with external services---such as preprocessing components, text annotators, or \acp{ui}---and assume interoperability through the GateJSON format. We also recall the design guideline of ensuring loose coupling among services to reduce cascading failures.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{\Acl{ie} flow.}
External services process raw data, serialize it in GateJSON, and inject it into the \ac{dia} via \acp{api}. This corresponds to the green arrows in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}, incoming from outside to the core of the \ac{dia}.

\item \emph{\Acl{ia} flow.}
\acp{ui} retrieve stored information in GateJSON via read or retrieval \acp{api} and present it to users. This corresponds to the outgoing red arrow in \Cref{fig:architectural-model}.

\item \emph{Circular flow.}
Some services both read from and write to the \ac{dia}, such as sequential processors that enrich documents (e.g.\ by adding annotation sets). The \emph{Error-correction \ac{ui}} is similar: it reads from the \ac{dia} and persists human-made revisions back into it.
\end{enumerate}

These three flows enable external services to ingest, access, and iteratively refine information, allowing the \ac{dia} to generalize across use cases. Moreover, running processing on top of previously generated results, combined with network-based \acp{api}, enables complex workflows and distributed execution. Operating on separate annotation sets minimizes conflicts among concurrent processes, and loose coupling supports scalability when combined with an orchestrator. The systematic use of \acp{uri} ensures that documents and annotations can be retrieved unambiguously via the \acp{api}.
