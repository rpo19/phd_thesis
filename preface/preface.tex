In recent years, \ac{nlp}---the field studying automatic processing and understanding of natural language---has been revolutionized by the transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}. Introduced in 2017, transformers improved both performance and training-time parallelization compared to previous approaches~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}, enabling researchers to train bigger models on huge natural language corpora, for later \say{transferring} the generic linguistic knowledge acquired to specific downstream tasks~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5}. This technique is known as \emph{transfer learning}~\cite{torrey2010transfer} and allows to reduce the dependence on large labeled data~\cite{zhuang_survey_transfer_learning_2021}, therefore improving the efficiency of the learning process~\cite{torrey2010transfer}. Examples of downstream tasks that can be enhanced with generic linguistic knowledge~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5} include: \emph{\acf{ner}}, the task of identifying mentions of entities like persons, locations, or organizations~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog,keraghel2024recentadvancesnamedentity,jm3}; \emph{\acf{qa}}, the task of understanding and responding to natural language questions~\cite{bates1995models}; and \emph{sentiment analysis}, which classifies texts as expressing positive or negative opinions~\cite{jm3}.

In the same year, OpenAI released ChatGPT~\cite{openai2025chatgpt}, bringing the advances of \acp{llm} to the general public through a chat-based \acf{ui}, and providing \acp{api} that enabled researchers, practitioners, and businesses to study the capabilities of instruction-tuned models, and integrate them into applications. Since its launch, ChatGPT's user base has grown rapidly, reaching 800 million users per week, approximately 10\% of the world's population~\cite{Sor2025chatgpt}.

\medskip\noindent
Despite their usefulness, \acp{llm} introduce non-negligible risks when deployed without safeguards, such as \emph{misuse} and \emph{misinformation}~\cite{bender2021stochastic,weidinger2022taxonomy}. They have been used as sources of factual knowledge despite being prone to \emph{hallucination}~\cite{maynez-etal-2020-faithfulness-hallucination,ji_etal-2023-survey-hallucination}---\ie the generation of incoherent, nonsensical, or unfaithful content. This risk is not merely hypothetical: in 2023, invented case law generated by ChatGPT was submitted and presented as authentic judicial decisions, an episode that resulted in formal sanctions by the court~\cite{milmo2023two_us_lawyers_fined_chatgpt}.
A second risk concerns environmental impact: modern \acp{llm} require substantial computation for both training and inference, leading to high energy consumption and a significant carbon footprint~\cite{strubell2019energy,patterson2022carbon}.

Also, in sensitive domains such as law or healthcare, documents often contain personal data that cannot be transferred to external servers. Using API-based \acp{llm} raises compliance issues under the European \emph{General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, Regulation (EU) 2016/679)}~\cite{gdpr2016} and \emph{Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)}~\cite{ai_act}. The GDPR~\cite{gdpr2016} imposes strict requirements on lawful and transparent data processing, while the \ac{ai} Act~\cite{ai_act} classifies legal and law enforcement applications as high-risk, requiring traceability, security, and human oversight. These obligations are difficult to meet when processing occurs on third-party infrastructures. Conversely, local deployment allows institutions to retain control of the data, simplifying compliance and safeguarding confidentiality.

Besides being sensitive in terms of data protection and confidentiality, the legal and healthcare sectors are also domains that require specialized expertise, deep understanding, and extensive information processing. Decisions in these fields rely heavily on expert knowledge and the careful interpretation of complex information sources. Another example of such a domain is finance, where similar forms of domain-specific expertise and reasoning are essential~\cite{niu-etal-2025-tree,cambridge-knowledge-intensive,kg-intensive-nlu-xai-2021}. In the remainder of this work, we refer to these as \emph{knowledge-intensive domains}, encompassing areas in which effective decision-making depends on the ability to access, interpret, and integrate heterogeneous and highly specialized information sources.

%\paragraph{NLP in knowledge-intensive domains}
Several approaches based on \acfp{nlp} techniques have been developed to support and enhance information workflows in knowledge-intensive domains. In these areas, professionals often face the challenge of locating, interpreting, and synthesizing large volumes of domain-specific information to make informed decisions. For instance, the need to retrieve precedent cases in law or identify relevant clinical trials in healthcare has driven the development of domain-specific \acf{ir} systems~\cite{moens2001innovative,breuker2004legal,hersh2003trec,roberts2016overview}. Other works have focused on automatic summarization to handle lengthy and complex documents~\cite{llms_in_law_2025,clinical_summarization_2023}, or on predictive modeling---such as drug response prediction in healthcare~\cite{sharma2024advances} and civil case outcome prediction in the legal domain~\cite{juliahb2024}. In finance, \ac{nlp} methods have been applied to the analysis of financial news, corporate announcements, and social media posts, supporting downstream tasks such as financial sentiment analysis and market forecasting~\cite{nlp_finance_survey_2025}.
% In scientific research, \ac{ir} and \ac{ie} systems assist in literature discovery, citation prediction, and \acl{kg} construction from scientific papers~\cite{}.
% These systems are designed to support complex queries and integrate multiple heterogeneous sources of information to aid expert decision-making~\cite{voorhees2014clinical,liu2023domain}.

%\paragraph{narrow to the legal}
Even though different knowledge-intensive domains have their own specific characteristics, certain recurring features can be observed across them. These include the central roles of entities---such as parties, judges, and lawyers in the legal domain; patients, doctors, and clinical staff in healthcare; and companies, investors, and regulators in finance---as well as the reliance on heterogeneous information sources. In the legal domain, these sources include court documents, such as civil court judgments, notary documents, or other contracts~\cite{HAN2024121052legalasst,amato2008,buey2016}. In the context of criminal investigations, it might be necessary to analyze chat messages extracted from suspects' mobile phone~\cite{subbulakshmi2024forensic,pirzada2024framework,kaushik2022forensic,Sonakshi_whatsapp_group_2025},  bank statements~\cite{data_mining_money_laundering_2003}, emails, or posts on social media platforms~\cite{jarrett2021impact}. In healthcare, relevant sources consist of laboratory reports~\cite{lab_reports_2023}, clinical trials, electronic health records, medical imaging, and data from health monitoring devices~\cite{XIAO2024108577}, while financial professionals consult financial news, statements, and market data~\cite{nlp_finance_survey_2025}.

\section{Legal Domain and Use Cases}
In the rest of this thesis, we focus on different use cases within the legal domain, a broad and knowledge-intensive area that shares many characteristics with other sensitive contexts such as healthcare. Specifically, we examine civil trials and criminal investigations through two representative types of sources in Italian: civil court judgments and investigative chat logs.

Civil court judgments are formal acts issued by the judge at the conclusion of a civil proceeding, constituting the official expression of the court's decision. They typically identify the parties involved, outline the relevant facts and procedural background, and present the legal reasoning supporting the decision, followed by the operative part specifying its effects. Their structure and mandatory elements are established by the Italian Code of Civil Procedure~\cite[Art.~132]{CPC}, which requires, among other things, the indication of the court and the parties, the statement of facts, and the reasoning in fact and law.

Investigative chat logs consist of messages extracted from \acp{ima}, such as WhatsApp\footnote{\url{https://www.whatsapp.com/}}, from the mobile phones of suspects during criminal investigations. These logs may include textual conversations, exchanged multimedia files, and metadata~\cite{P_rez_2021}. In the forensic context, such data are carefully collected and analyzed to reconstruct events~\cite{P_rez_2021}, identify relationships among participants~\cite{Sonakshi_whatsapp_group_2025}, for gathering evidence in support of the proceeding~\cite{pirzada2024framework}.

%\paragraph{legal challenges}
However, applying \ac{nlp} techniques to the legal domain entails specific challenges, including regulatory constraints, evolving legislation, domain-specific language, and legal interpretations that differ from general usage.
%
For instance, in tasks such as estimating the similarity between different legal proceedings, using general-domain methods for document similarity may incorrectly label two cases as similar, even when the law treats them very differently. For example, the theft of similar products can be judged under different laws depending on the value of the stolen items~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}, potentially leading to very different penalties.

The concept of similarity between legal cases also strongly depends on the outcome of the trials~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}, which may be overlooked by general-domain \ac{nlp} approaches.
Moreover, this domain is characterized by a peculiar language that follows implicit structure, which may however vary depending on the country, on the court, or on the legal professional that is writing the document~\cite{SANSONE2022101967,breit_legal_permits_2020}. The complexity of the legal language is further reflected in the nature of legal documents themselves, which are often lengthy and heterogeneous~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}.

Other challenges include compliance with regulations, as described above, as well as the evolving nature of the legal framework. Statutes may change over time, meaning that cases previously considered similar may no longer be so under current legislation~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. In addition, legal documents are structured into multiple sections serving distinct purposes~\cite[Art.~132]{CPC}, and the relevance of each section depends on the analytical task~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. For example, when identifying similar cases, the comparison should focus on the factual and legal reasoning sections, which contain the elements that determine the applicability of legal provisions. In contrast, when searching for precedents involving the same individuals or organizations, the relevant information may appear primarily in the introductory sections describing the parties. Models that ignore this internal structure and treat the text as a single unstructured block risk misleading results---for instance, ranking two cases as similar simply because they mention the same persons, even though the underlying legal issues and outcomes differ substantially.

\noindent
Despite these challenges, advances in \ac{ai} and \ac{nlp} have greatly expanded the potential for content analysis. Yet, in professional and institutional contexts, the ultimate goal is not analysis itself, but the ability to find, interpret, and use information to support decision-making. In such domains, \ac{ai} technologies are most valuable when they enhance users’ \emph{information access}~\cite{human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}, understood here as the ability to effectively identify, retrieve, and utilize information. Over time, information access has evolved from keyword-based search and faceted navigation to more interactive paradigms, such as conversational systems and \acp{llm} that assist users in exploring and reasoning over complex data.

Considering the challenges discussed above, it becomes essential to examine how professionals in the legal and investigative domains actually access and use information in their daily work.
To illustrate these needs, we identify a set of representative use cases of \acf{ia}, which highlight typical queries, document navigation, and analytical activities.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textit{UC~\arabic*:},ref=\textit{UC~\arabic*}]
    \item \label{uc:case-retrieval} \emph{Case retrieval and precedent search:} Judges and lawyers may need to search for previous cases involving the same individuals or organizations~\cite{human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}, or analyze outcomes of similar cases to identify legal trends to ensure uniformity in the interpretation of the law~\cite{egea2024does}.
    \item \label{uc:navigation} \emph{Document navigation and content exploration:} When reviewing lengthy judgments, legal professionals benefit from tools that allow them to quickly identify specific information or sections~\cite{swoopes2025interfacedesignsupportlegal,ziwei_abstractexplorer_2025,raymond_skimming_sci_2023}, for example by locating all mentions of a given person.
    \item \label{uc:investigative-retrieval} \emph{Investigative retrieval:} Investigators may need to retrieve specific documents from a large corpus from seized devices~\cite{BEEBE200749}, identify chat messages to understand real events~\cite{pirzada2024framework}---such as to confirm a meeting between suspects---or quickly visualize the communication network of the individuals under investigation~\cite{Sonakshi_whatsapp_group_2025}.
    \item \label{uc:qa} \emph{Question answering on documents and collections:} In the \acf{ai} era, when search engines can answer questions directly in natural language~\cite{Google2024generative_search}, legal professionals can benefit from \acf{qa} systems that provide answers on a single document or across document collections, along with references to the supporting evidence~\cite{HAN2024121052legalasst,}.
    \item \label{uc:stats} \emph{Statistical analysis and monitoring:} Authorities, such as the Ministry of Justice, may require aggregated statistics, \eg the number of new cases per court per year or the average trial duration, to support performance monitoring, policy assessment, and resource planning~\cite{bellandi2025streamlining}.
\end{enumerate}

%\paragraph{motivation}
These use cases reveal a recurrent pattern: professionals operate on large and
heterogeneous corpora, leading to time-consuming~\cite{pirzada2024framework}
and often manual workflows~\cite{buey2016}, which slows down legal and
investigative analysis. There is therefore a strong motivation to design \acl{ia}
methods and infrastructures that can accelerate these tasks---\eg through
faceted navigation, entity-based retrieval, question answering, and statistical
exploration.

\section{Challenges and Objectives}

The use cases introduced above highlight how \ac{ai} and \ac{nlp} can support professionals in fulfilling their information needs in the legal and investigative contexts.  
However, developing such systems remains far from trivial. In practice, several key challenges must be addressed to ensure that these technologies are effective, reliable, and legally compliant.  
The main challenges motivating this thesis can be summarized as follows:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textit{Ch.~\arabic*:},ref=\textit{Ch.~\arabic*}]
    \item \label{ch:scalability} \emph{Scalability.} Legal and investigative corpora may include thousands of heterogeneous documents, requiring methods that can efficiently process and integrate large volumes of text and metadata.
    \item \label{ch:heterogeneity} \emph{Heterogeneity.} The data vary widely in format and structure---from judgments and contracts to chat logs and structured evidence---which demands for flexible representations capable of integrating diverse sources within a unified framework.
    \item \label{ch:traceability} \emph{Traceability and verifiability.} The outputs of \ac{ai}-driven systems must remain interpretable and auditable: each extracted or inferred element should be traceable back to its source document, supporting transparency and human oversight~\cite[Art.~14--15]{ai_act}.
    \item \label{ch:nilintegration} \emph{Integration of novel entities.}
    Legal and investigative documents---as well as those from other sensitive domains such as healthcare---often mention entities that are not publicly known or documented in existing \acl{kr} (\eg Wikipedia or Wikidata). Handling such \emph{novel} (or \emph{NIL}) entities is therefore a key challenge, as it requires extending the knowledge representation to include new individuals emerging from the data, while maintaining consistency and interoperability with existing resources.
\end{enumerate}

These challenges highlight the need for architectural solutions that can organize, connect, and make sense of heterogeneous data while preserving interpretability and compliance.


\subsection{Research hypothesis}



Across the diverse approaches reviewed in \Cref{chap:background}, a common principle emerges: the design of an intermediate architecture that mediates between users and the underlying sources of information. Such architectures may take the form of search engines indexing documents for efficient retrieval; visualization systems that highlight relevant elements in user interfaces~\cite{swoopes2025interfacedesignsupportlegal,ziwei_abstractexplorer_2025,raymond_skimming_sci_2023,pirzada2024framework,P_rez_2021,kejriwal2018}; or data infrastructures such as knowledge graphs and databases~\cite{kejriwal2017searchengine,kejriwal2018,szekely2015building,amato2008,HAN2024121052legalasst,bellandi2025streamlining}. These systems share the goal of organizing and presenting information in a form that is meaningful to users and suited to their analytical tasks.

As illustrated in \Cref{fig:overview}, the architecture proposed in this thesis serves as a bridge between extraction and access, enabling the transition from unstructured data to semantically organized, queryable knowledge. It supports both formal legal documents (such as judgments and acts) and heterogeneous data produced in investigative contexts, providing the foundation for the use cases discussed above.



Building on this idea, this thesis is grounded on the hypothesis that \emph{an entity-centric architecture can effectively integrate heterogeneous legal and investigative data, enabling advanced forms of information access and analysis while preserving scalability and traceability}. In particular, such an architecture would support the identified use cases in the legal and investigative domains. For instance, users may perform faceted search to filter relevant documents or messages based on entities such as persons, locations, or dates, ask questions in natural language to retrieve precise information, and even combine these capabilities---for example, applying question answering over a subset of documents filtered with faceted search.



Entities serve as conceptual anchors that connect information within and across documents, enabling cross-references and semantic aggregation. They also enhance generalizability, as entities are common across domains: examples include persons, judges, and legal acts in law; patients, diseases, and treatments in healthcare; or companies, investors, and assets in finance. As illustrated in \Cref{fig:entities_central}, entities act as the connective tissue linking diverse data types---ranging from structured bank transfers to unstructured reports, voice messages, and chat transcripts---and support aggregation, such as computing counts or groupings over certain entities or entity types, as well as faceting, that is, organizing retrieved items along multiple dimensions to support interactive filtering and exploration~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted}.

\subsection{Research objectives and contributions}

In response to the identified challenges and building on the hypothesis that an entity-centric architecture can integrate heterogeneous data and support advanced composable forms of \acl{ia}, this thesis develops entity-oriented strategies that connect information extraction and access in knowledge-intensive domains, with a focus on the legal and investigative settings.  
These strategies are articulated through the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textit{Obj.~\arabic*:},ref=\textit{Obj.~\arabic*}]

    \item \label{ob:extraction_assessment} \textbf{Assessment of existing \acl{ie} technologies considering legal domain challenges.}  
    The first objective is to empirically evaluate the adaptability of existing \acl{ie} components---including \acl{ner}, \acl{nel}, and NIL prediction---to legal and investigative data, accounting for domain-specific constraints, linguistic heterogeneity, and the occurrence of novel entities (also called NIL entities).

    This assessment provided quantitative evidence on domain adaptation needs and showed that handling NIL entities is prone to error propagation, with NIL prediction being a major source of errors. Existing \acl{ee} models can be applied to legal documents and investigative chats, but achieving satisfactory quality often requires in-domain fine-tuning or \acl{hitl} correction.


    \item \label{ob:architecture} \textbf{Design of an entity-centric architecture for data integration and \acl{ia}.}
    The second objective is the definition of an architecture that organizes heterogeneous and unstructured information around entities (\eg persons, locations, organizations, or laws), bridging information extraction and access.
    
    The designed architecture enables the development of applications for advanced use cases---such as those considered in this thesis---and supports their eventual combination, for example by performing question answering after faceted search.

    \item \label{ob:refactx} \textbf{Development of an efficient and scalable \acl{qa} system grounded in external knowledge.}  
    The third objective is to design a \acl{qa} system capable of producing answers that are explicitly supported by evidence from the knowledge base, thus enabling verification and mitigating hallucination risks.  
    The system should scale to large collections and operate efficiently in local environments, in compliance with regulatory and confidentiality constraints.  

    To achieve these goals, the thesis introduces \refactx, a constrained-generation approach that efficiently guides an \ac{llm} through a disk-backed prefix tree to produce valid facts directly from large \acp{kb}, without the need for retrievers, complex pipelines, or architectural modifications to the underlying \ac{llm}.


\end{enumerate}

This \phd thesis is organized as follows. \Cref{chap:background} presents the background concepts required for this work, including \aclp{llm} and an overview of the Italian legal system. \Cref{chap:sota} reviews the state of the art relevant to the three research objectives: information extraction (including \acl{ner}, \acl{nel}, and novel entities), data-integration architectures for knowledge-intensive domains, and \acl{qa}. \Cref{chap:extraction} addresses \ref{ob:extraction_assessment}, \Cref{chap:architecture} addresses \ref{ob:architecture}, and \Cref{chap:refactx} addresses \ref{ob:refactx}. Finally, \Cref{chap:conclusion} concludes the thesis.
