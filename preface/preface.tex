Knowledge-intensive domains are fields that demand specialized expertise, deep understanding, and extensive information processing, such as law, healthcare, finance, and scientific research~\cite{niu-etal-2025-tree,cambridge-knowledge-intensive}. In these areas, decisions rely heavily on the expert's domain knowledge~\cite{kg-intensive-nlu-xai-2021}. To gather the required knowledge, professionals may need to consult a variety of sources, such as clinical records, research papers, and medical guidelines in healthcare, or legal codes, court decisions, contracts, and investigative or forensic reports in law. 

Several \acf{nlp} approaches have been introduced for improving the workflow in these domains. The need for retrieving precedent cases in law or identifying relevant clinical trials in healthcare motivated the development of domain-specific \acf{ir} systems~\cite{moens2001innovative,breuker2004legal,hersh2003trec,roberts2016overview}. Document summarization systems have been introduced for lengthy documents~\cite{llms_in_law_2025,clinical_summarization_2023}, or predictive methods---such as drug response prediction in the healthcare domain~\cite{sharma2024advances}, or civil case outcome prediction in the legal domain~\cite{juliahb2024}.
% These systems are designed to support complex queries and integrate multiple heterogeneous sources of information to aid expert decision-making~\cite{voorhees2014clinical,liu2023domain}.

In this thesis, we consider the legal domain as representative of knowledge-intensive domains, especially focusing on civil court judgments---i.e., the documents which conclude a civil trial and contains the parties, the facts, and the motivated final decision---and investigative chat logs---i.e., the chat messages extracted from seized suspects' phones during an investigation.

However, the legal domain is characterized by specific challenges. According to the law, the theft of similar products may be regulated with different law articles, according to the price of the stolen item~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. Consequently, two cases that appear similar according to general-domain \ac{nlp} tools may be considered quite different in the eyes of the law. The concept of similarity between legal cases also heavily depends on the outcome of the trials~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}, which may be overlooked by general-domain \ac{nlp} methods.
Furthermore, this domain is characterized by a peculiar language that follows implicit structure, which however can vary depending on the country, on the court, or on the professionist that is writing the document~\cite{SANSONE2022101967,breit_legal_permits_2020}. Documents are often long in size and heterogeneous in nature~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}---for example, the data acquired during an investigation may include legal contracts, chat messages, multimodal posts on social media, pictures, and audio recordings.

Another important challenge for using \ac{nlp} systems, or other automated tools in this domain, is the compliance with the regulations, such as respecting principles like transparency, non-discrimination, and privacy~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}\todo{magari cita direttamente regulations}.

This work focuses on the \acl{ia} needs of users in knowledge-intensive domains, with particular attention to the legal domain. These needs encompass retrieving relevant documents, efficiently exploring their content, posing questions about individual documents or entire collections, and computing statistics across document corpora.

% casi d'uso
For example, judges and lawyers may need to search and consult previous cases involving the same individuals or analyze the outcomes of similar cases to identify legal trends. When reviewing lengthy judgments, they can benefit from tools that allow them to quickly navigate to specific sections or locate all mentions of a given person. Investigators, on the other hand, may need to retrieve a particular contract from a seized hard drive or identify chat messages confirming a meeting between suspects. Moreover, in the \acf{ai} era, when popular search engines are able to detect and directly answer some questions in natural language, legal professionals may benefit from \acf{qa} systems that directly answer questions on the document they are reading or on document collections, additionally providing references to the pieces of information supporting the answers.
Finally, authorities such as the Ministry of Justice may need to compute statistics, including the number of new cases per court each year or the average trial duration by court, for performance monitoring and policy assessment~\cite{bellandi2025streamlining}.

Several systems for assisting legal professionals have been developed over time~\cite{amato2008,buey2016,breit_legal_permits_2020,HAN2024121052legalasst,human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}.
Early work by \textcite{amato2008} introduced a document management system for the legal domain that leverages ontologies and entity extraction, focusing primarily on notary documents.
\textcite{buey2016} focused on a wider range of legal documents---such as public acts and private agreements---by employing ontologies that model document structures and specify which entities should be extracted from each section.
\textcite{breit_legal_permits_2020} later explored the automation of processes related to legal permits\todo{add description once verified}.
More recent work include \textcite{HAN2024121052legalasst,human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}. The former proposed \emph{LegalAsst}, a system designed to enhance court productivity by integrating \acp{llm}, entity recognition, graph-based visualizations of trial facts, and decision-tree representations of judicial reasoning.
The latter developed a prototype for legal case retrieval that guides professionals interactively through a visualized faceted search interface, recommending alternative search terms to support precedent discovery.

On the other hand, approaches supporting criminal investigations have targeted illicit Web data~\cite{kejriwal2017searchengine,kejriwal2018}, particularly in efforts to combat human trafficking. These works propose an entity-centric scalable architecture that constructs a knowledge graph---according to a domain-specific ontology---and provides advanced \acl{ia} \acp{ui}, such as faceted search interfaces and map-based visualizations. Although the architecture could be generalized to other types of documents---with a different ontology---they did no experiment with multimedia data and implement the extraction services for specific challenges of illicit data, such as information that is often hidden in plain sight, such as phone numbers deliberately obfuscated by mixing digits and words.

\textcite{P_rez_2021} proposed a modular platform for analyzing vast and heterogeneous data---including multimedia content---from multiple sources. Their system is built upon an ontology composed by the entities \texttt{Person}, \texttt{Location}, \texttt{Organization}, \texttt{Equipment}, and \texttt{Event}, and adopts a scalable microservices-based architecture~\cite{microservices_2016} that integrates components such as speech-to-text and keyword extraction modules to populate a \ac{kb}.
