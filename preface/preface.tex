In recent years, \ac{nlp}---the field studying automatic processing and understanding of natural language---has been revolutionized by the transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}. Introcuded in 2017, transformers improved performance and training-time parallelization with respect to previous architectures~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}, enabling research to train bigger models on huge natural language corpora, for later \say{transfering} the generic linguistic knowledge acquired to specific downstream tasks~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5}. This technique is known as \emph{transfer learning}~\cite{torrey2010transfer} and allows to reduce the dependence on large labeled data~\cite{zhuang_survey_transfer_learning_2021}, therefore improving the efficiency of the learning process~\cite{torrey2010transfer}. Examples of downstream tasks that can be enhanced with generic linguistic knowledge~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5} include \ac{ner}---the task of identifying mentions of entities like perons, locations, or organizations~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog,keraghel2024recentadvancesnamedentity,jm3}---question answering---the task of understanding and responding to natural language questions~\cite{bates1995models}---or sentiment analysis---which, given a text, classifies whether the writer expressed a positive or negative sentiment, where positive could be ``Had a great experience eating here.'' and negative ``The service was awful.'' for a resturant review~\cite{jm3}.

Subsequently, tranformer-based \acp{lm} have been scaled to billions of parameters---GPT3, released in 2020, counted 175 billion parameters---demostrating impressive performance even without fine-tuning~\cite{browngpt3}. The real revolution, however, came in 2022 with \emph{instruction tuning}~\cite{instruct_gpt_2022} that allowed to align \acp{llm} to users' intent improving their capability of following instructions while remaining truthful and minimizing bias, toxicity, or other harmful behaviors~\cite{instruct_gpt_2022}. In the same year, ChatGPT~\cite{openai2025chatgpt} was released, bringing the advances of \acp{llm} to the general public through a chat-based \ac{ui}, and providing \acp{api} that enabled researchers, practitioners, and businesses to study the capabilities of instruction-tuned models, and integrate them into applications. Since its launch, ChatGPT's user base has grown rapidly, reaching 800 million users per week, approximately 10\% of the world’s population~\cite{Sor2025chatgpt}.

Today, even popular search engines, such as Google Search\footnote{\url{https://www.google.com/}} have integrated \acp{llm} to provide quick answers or \ac{ai} overviews of the search results~\cite{Google2024generative_search}, as visible in \Cref{fig:actor-search}.

Current state-of-the-art \acp{llm} count hundreds of billions of parameters---an example is DeepSeek-V3~\cite{deepseekai2025deepseekv3technicalreport} whose 671 billions parameters are available on HuggingFace\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V3}}---and are generally served as \acp{api} due to the high hardware requrements derived from their size.
Indeed, serving DeepSeek-V3 on \acp{gpu}---which are used for \acp{llm} because they provide massive parallelism and high throughput for matrix multiplications---would normally require loading all 671 billion parameters into \ac{gpu} memory. Assuming each parameter is represented using 4 bytes (32 bits), the memory requirement can be estimated as:
\[
\text{Memory} = 671 \times 10^9 \text{ parameters} \times 4 \text{ bytes/parameter} = 2.684 \times 10^{12} \text{ bytes} \approx 2.68 \text{ TB}.
\]
Consumer-grade \acp{gpu}, such as the 2{,}000~\euro{} Nvidia RTX~5090\footnote{\url{https://www.nvidia.com/it-it/geforce/graphics-cards/50-series/rtx-5090/}}, featuring 32~GB of memory, allow to load approximately 8~billion parameters in 32-bit precision, or up to 64~billion parameters in 4-bit precision. This makes such hardware suitable for running small or medium-sized \acp{llm}, but far from sufficient for hosting state-of-the-art \acp{llm}.

Also, in sensitive domains such as legal or healthcare, where documents often contain personal data, \ac{api}-based \ac{ai} models cannot always be used, since they typically require transferring data to external servers operated by third-party providers. This can conflict with the principles of data protection and confidentiality established by the European legal framework. Under the \emph{General Data Protection Regulation} (GDPR, Regulation (EU) 2016/679), personal data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently~\cite[Art.~5]{gdpr2016}, and the controller---that is, the entity determining the purposes and means of processing~\cite[Art.~4.7}{gdpr2016}---must ensure that data are not transferred outside the European Economic Area unless an adequate level of protection is guaranteed~\cite[Art.~44--49]{gdpr2016}. Furthermore, data revealing legal matters, health status, or other sensitive attributes fall under the \emph{special categories of personal data}~\cite[Art.~9]{gdpr2016}, which require explicit consent or a specific legal basis for processing. The use of external \acp{api} may therefore compromise compliance with data minimization and purpose limitation principles~\cite[Art.~5.1.c--b]{gdpr2016}, as data are sent to infrastructures beyond the deployer's control. Using such \acp{api} with personal data is legally complex: a lawful basis (e.g., consent or public interest) must be established~\cite[Art.~6 and~9]{gdpr2016}, a data processing agreement must define roles and safeguards~\cite[Art.~28]{gdpr2016}, and for those \ac{api} providers that process data outside the EU additional transfer obligations are required~\cite[Art.~44--49]{gdpr2016}. Consequently, it is difficult to rely on API-based models without prior anonymization or pseudonymization of the input data.

The \emph{Artificial Intelligence Act} (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)~\cite{ai_act} reinforces these obligations for \ac{ai} systems operating in high-risk sectors, such as healthcare and the administration of justice~\cite[Annex~III]{ai_act}. Providers and deployers of high-risk \ac{ai} systems must implement appropriate risk management, data governance, and logging mechanisms~\cite[Art.~9--12]{ai_act}, and ensure that the system operates in a manner that preserves data security and traceability~\cite[Art.~15]{ai_act}. In this context, the deployer---that is, the natural or legal person using an \ac{ai} system under their authority (i.e., within their organization or responsibility)~\cite[Art.~3.4]{ai_act}---is responsible for ensuring that the system's operation complies with the \ac{ai} Act within its specific use case. When an \ac{ai} model is accessed via a remote \ac{api}, these safeguards depend on the provider's infrastructure, making it difficult for the deployer to demonstrate compliance. Locally deployed models, instead, allow organizations to retain full control over the data processing environment, enforce internal access policies, and ensure that confidential or sensitive information remains within the institution's secure perimeter. This makes local deployment a preferable option in legally or ethically sensitive domains.

Besides being sensitive domains, law and healthcare are also knowledge-intensive, like finance and scientific research~\cite{niu-etal-2025-tree,cambridge-knowledge-intensive}, requiring specialized expertise, deep understanding, and extensive information processing. In these areas, decisions rely heavily on expert knowledge~\cite{kg-intensive-nlu-xai-2021}, and professionals may need to consult diverse sources, such as clinical records, research papers, and medical guidelines in healthcare; legal codes, court decisions, contracts, and investigative or forensic reports in law; financial statements, market analyses, and regulatory filings in finance; and academic journals, experimental data, and research protocols in scientific research.

Several \acf{nlp} approaches have been introduced for improving the workflow in these domains. The need for retrieving precedent cases in law or identifying relevant clinical trials in healthcare motivated the development of domain-specific \acf{ir} systems~\cite{moens2001innovative,breuker2004legal,hersh2003trec,roberts2016overview}. Document summarization systems have been introduced for lengthy documents~\cite{llms_in_law_2025,clinical_summarization_2023}, or predictive methods---such as drug response prediction in the healthcare domain~\cite{sharma2024advances}, or civil case outcome prediction in the legal domain~\cite{juliahb2024}.
% These systems are designed to support complex queries and integrate multiple heterogeneous sources of information to aid expert decision-making~\cite{voorhees2014clinical,liu2023domain}.

In this thesis, we consider the legal domain as representative of knowledge-intensive domains, especially focusing on civil court judgments---i.e., the documents which conclude a civil trial and contains the parties, the facts, and the motivated final decision---and investigative chat logs---i.e., the chat messages extracted from seized suspects' phones during an investigation.

However, the legal domain is characterized by specific challenges. According to the law, the theft of similar products may be regulated with different law articles with respect to the price of the stolen item~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. Consequently, two cases that appear similar according to general-domain \ac{nlp} tools may be considered quite different in the eyes of the law. The concept of similarity between legal cases also heavily depends on the outcome of the trials~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}, which may be overlooked by general-domain \ac{nlp} methods.
Furthermore, this domain is characterized by a peculiar language that follows implicit structure, which however can vary depending on the country, on the court, or on the professionist that is writing the document~\cite{SANSONE2022101967,breit_legal_permits_2020}. Documents are often long in size and heterogeneous in nature~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}---for example, the data acquired during an investigation may include legal contracts, chat messages, multimodal posts on social media, pictures, and audio recordings.

Another important challenge for using \ac{nlp} systems, or other automated tools in this domain, is the compliance with the regulations, such as respecting principles like transparency, non-discrimination, and privacy~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}\todo{magari cita direttamente regulations}.

This work focuses on the \acl{ia} needs of users in knowledge-intensive domains, with particular attention to the legal domain. These needs encompass retrieving relevant documents, efficiently exploring their content, posing questions about individual documents or entire collections, and computing statistics across document corpora.

% casi d'uso
For example, judges and lawyers may need to search and consult previous cases involving the same individuals or analyze the outcomes of similar cases to identify legal trends. When reviewing lengthy judgments, they can benefit from tools that allow them to quickly navigate to specific sections or locate all mentions of a given person. Investigators, on the other hand, may need to retrieve a particular contract from a seized hard drive or identify chat messages confirming a meeting between suspects. Moreover, in the \acf{ai} era, when popular search engines directly answer some questions in natural language, legal professionals may benefit from \acf{qa} systems that directly answer questions on the document they are reading or on document collections, additionally providing references to the pieces of information supporting the answers.
Finally, authorities such as the Ministry of Justice may need to compute statistics, including the number of new cases per court each year or the average trial duration by court, for performance monitoring and policy assessment~\cite{bellandi2025streamlining}.

Several systems for assisting legal professionals have been developed over time~\cite{amato2008,buey2016,breit_legal_permits_2020,HAN2024121052legalasst,human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}.
Early work by \textcite{amato2008} introduced a document management system for the legal domain that leverages ontologies and entity extraction, focusing primarily on notary documents.
\textcite{buey2016} focused on a wider range of legal documents---such as public acts and private agreements---by employing ontologies that model document structures and specify which entities should be extracted from each section.
\textcite{breit_legal_permits_2020} later explored the automation of processes related to legal permits\todo{add description once verified}.

The former proposed \emph{LegalAsst}, a system designed to enhance court productivity by integrating \acp{llm}, entity recognition, graph-based visualizations of trial facts, and decision-tree representations of judicial reasoning.
The latter developed a prototype for legal case retrieval that guides professionals interactively through a visualized faceted search interface, recommending alternative search terms to support precedent discovery.

On the other hand, approaches supporting criminal investigations have targeted illicit Web data~\cite{kejriwal2017searchengine,kejriwal2018}, particularly in efforts to combat human trafficking. These works propose an entity-centric scalable architecture that constructs a knowledge graph---according to a domain-specific ontology---and provides advanced \acl{ia} \acp{ui}, such as faceted search interfaces and map-based visualizations. Although the architecture could be generalized to other types of documents---with a different ontology---they did no experiment with multimedia data and implement the extraction services for specific challenges of illicit data, such as information that is often hidden in plain sight, such as phone numbers deliberately obfuscated by mixing digits and words.
% both focus also on scalability
\textcite{P_rez_2021} proposed a modular platform for analyzing vast and heterogeneous data---including multimedia content---from multiple sources. Their system is built upon an ontology composed by the entities \texttt{Person}, \texttt{Location}, \texttt{Organization}, \texttt{Equipment}, and \texttt{Event}, and adopts a scalable microservices-based architecture~\cite{microservices_2016} that integrates components such as speech-to-text and keyword extraction modules to populate a \ac{kb}. While the architecture is both modular and generalizable, their experiments rely primarily on keyword-based extraction techniques, which provide limited semantic understanding and require manual intervention.

Furthermore, these approaches for enhancing criminal investigations do not address \ac{kc} processes, such as \ac{nel}, except for specific applications like clustering entities via phone numbers~\cite{kejriwal2017searchengine}. This results in the risk of ambiguity---for example, a keyword-based system may identify ``Lisbon'' without distinguishing between \texttt{Lisbon, Capital of Portugal}, \texttt{Lisbon, Connecticut}, and \texttt{Lisbon (1956 film)}, or fail to recognize that ``the president of the European Commission'' and ``Ursula von der Leyen'' refer to the same entity. Incorporating \ac{nel} techniques would also enable systems to leverage existing knowledge repositories, such as Wikipedia~\cite{wikipedia:wikipedia}, or domain-specific ones, such as legal codes.
