In recent years, \ac{nlp}---the field studying automatic processing and understanding of natural language---has been revolutionized by the transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}. Introcuded in 2017, transformers improved performance and training-time parallelization with respect to previous architectures~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017}, enabling research to train bigger models on huge natural language corpora, for later \say{transfering} the generic linguistic knowledge acquired to specific downstream tasks~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5}. This technique is known as \emph{transfer learning}~\cite{torrey2010transfer} and allows to reduce the dependence on large labeled data~\cite{zhuang_survey_transfer_learning_2021}, therefore improving the efficiency of the learning process~\cite{torrey2010transfer}. Examples of downstream tasks that can be enhanced with generic linguistic knowledge~\cite{jm3,devlin-etal-2019-bert,radford2018improving,Raffel2020t5} include \ac{ner}---the task of identifying mentions of entities like perons, locations, or organizations~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog,keraghel2024recentadvancesnamedentity,jm3}---question answering---the task of understanding and responding to natural language questions~\cite{bates1995models}---or sentiment analysis---which, given a text, classifies whether the writer expressed a positive or negative sentiment, where positive could be ``Had a great experience eating here.'' and negative ``The service was awful.'' for a resturant review~\cite{jm3}.

Subsequently, tranformer-based \acp{lm} have been scaled to billions of parameters---GPT3, released in 2020, counted 175 billion parameters---demostrating impressive performance even without fine-tuning~\cite{browngpt3}. The real revolution, however, came in 2022 with \emph{instruction tuning}~\cite{instruct_gpt_2022} that allowed to align \acp{llm} to users' intent improving their capability of following instructions while remaining truthful and minimizing bias, toxicity, or other harmful behaviors~\cite{instruct_gpt_2022}. In the same year, OpenAI released ChatGPT~\cite{openai2025chatgpt}, bringing the advances of \acp{llm} to the general public through a chat-based \ac{ui}, and providing \acp{api} that enabled researchers, practitioners, and businesses to study the capabilities of instruction-tuned models, and integrate them into applications. Since its launch, ChatGPT's user base has grown rapidly, reaching 800 million users per week, approximately 10\% of the world’s population~\cite{Sor2025chatgpt}.

Current state-of-the-art \acp{llm} count hundreds of billions of parameters---an example is DeepSeek-V3~\cite{deepseekai2025deepseekv3technicalreport} whose 671 billions parameters are available on HuggingFace\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V3}}---and are generally served as \acp{api} due to the high hardware requrements derived from their size.
Indeed, serving DeepSeek-V3 on \acp{gpu}---which are used for \acp{llm} because they provide massive parallelism and high throughput for matrix multiplications---would normally require loading all 671 billion parameters into \ac{gpu} memory. Assuming each parameter is represented using 4 bytes (32 bits), the memory requirement can be estimated as:
\[
\text{Memory} = 671 \times 10^9 \text{ parameters} \times 4 \text{ bytes/parameter} = 2.684 \times 10^{12} \text{ bytes} \approx 2.68 \text{ TB}.
\]
Consumer-grade \acp{gpu}, such as the 2{,}000~\euro{} Nvidia RTX~5090\footnote{\url{https://www.nvidia.com/it-it/geforce/graphics-cards/50-series/rtx-5090/}}, featuring 32~GB of memory, allow to load approximately 8~billion parameters in 32-bit precision, or up to 64~billion parameters in 4-bit precision. This makes such hardware suitable for running small or medium-sized \acp{llm}, but far from sufficient for hosting state-of-the-art \acp{llm}.

Also, in sensitive domains such as legal or healthcare, where documents often contain personal data, \ac{api}-based \ac{ai} models cannot always be used, since they typically require transferring data to external servers operated by third-party providers. This can conflict with the principles of data protection and confidentiality established by the European legal framework. Under the \emph{General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, Regulation (EU) 2016/679)}~\cite{gdpr2016}, personal data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently~\cite[Art.~5]{gdpr2016}, and the controller---that is, the entity determining the purposes and means of processing~\cite[Art.~4.7]{gdpr2016}---must ensure that data are not transferred outside the European Economic Area unless an adequate level of protection is guaranteed~\cite[Art.~44--49]{gdpr2016}.
The use of external \acp{api} may therefore compromise compliance with data minimization and purpose limitation principles~\cite[Art.~5.1.c--b]{gdpr2016}, as data are sent to infrastructures beyond the deployer's control.
Under the \emph{Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)}~\cite{ai_act}, the provider is the entity that develops or markets the \ac{ai} system---for example, a company such as OpenAI offering a model accessible through an online \ac{api}~\cite[Art.~3.3]{ai_act}.
The deployer, instead, is the natural or legal person, public authority, or other body that uses the system under its authority~\cite[Art.~3.4]{ai_act}; in a legal setting, this could be a court or law enforcement agency that integrates the model into a software tool to support document review or investigations.
Using \acp{api} with personal data is legally complex: a lawful basis (e.g., consent or public interest) must be established~\cite[Art.~6 and~9]{gdpr2016}, a data processing agreement must define roles and safeguards~\cite[Art.~28.3]{gdpr2016}, and for those \ac{api} providers that process data outside the EU, additional transfer obligations apply~\cite[Art.~44--49]{gdpr2016}. Consequently, it is difficult to rely on API-based models without prior anonymization or pseudonymization of the input data.

The AI Act~\cite{ai_act} reinforces these obligations for \ac{ai} systems operating in high-risk sectors, such as law enforcement and the administration of justice~\cite[Annex~III]{ai_act}. Providers and deployers of high-risk \ac{ai} systems must implement appropriate risk management, data governance, and logging mechanisms~\cite[Art.~9--12]{ai_act}, and ensure that the system operates in a manner that preserves data security and traceability~\cite[Art.~15]{ai_act}. In this context, the deployer---for instance, a court or investigative authority using an \ac{ai} model to analyze case materials---is responsible for ensuring that the system's operation complies with the \ac{ai} Act within its specific use case. When an \ac{ai} model is accessed via a remote \ac{api}, these safeguards depend on the provider's infrastructure, making it difficult for the deployer to demonstrate compliance. Locally deployed models, instead, allow organizations to retain full control over the data processing environment, enforce internal access policies, and ensure that confidential or sensitive information remains within the institution's secure perimeter. This makes local deployment a preferable option for using \acp{llm} in the legal domain.

\paragraph{introduce kg intensive and start to move to the legal domain}
Besides being sensitive in terms of data protection and confidentiality, the legal and healthcare sectors are also domains that require specialized expertise, deep understanding, and extensive information processing. Decisions in these fields rely heavily on expert knowledge and the careful interpretation of complex information sources. Another example of such a domain is finance, where similar forms of domain-specific expertise and reasoning are essential~\cite{niu-etal-2025-tree,cambridge-knowledge-intensive,kg-intensive-nlu-xai-2021}. In the remaining of this work, we refer to these as \emph{knowledge-intensive domains}, encompassing areas in which effective decision-making depends on the ability to access, interpret, and integrate heterogeneous and highly specialized information sources.

\paragraph{NLP in knowledge-intensive domains}
Several \acf{nlp}-based approaches have been developed to support and enhance information workflows in knowledge-intensive domains. In these areas, professionals often face the challenge of locating, interpreting, and synthesizing large volumes of domain-specific information to make informed decisions. For instance, the need to retrieve precedent cases in law or identify relevant clinical trials in healthcare has driven the development of domain-specific \acf{ir} systems~\cite{moens2001innovative,breuker2004legal,hersh2003trec,roberts2016overview}. Other works have focused on automatic summarization to handle lengthy and complex documents~\cite{llms_in_law_2025,clinical_summarization_2023}, or on predictive modeling---such as drug response prediction in healthcare~\cite{sharma2024advances} and civil case outcome prediction in the legal domain~\cite{juliahb2024}. In finance, \ac{nlp} methods have been applied to the analysis of financial news, corporate announcements, and social media posts, supporting downstream tasks such as financial sentiment analysis and market forecasting~\cite{nlp_finance_survey_2025}.

Even though different knowledge-intensive domains have their own specific characteristics, certain recurring features can be observed across them. These include the central roles of entities---such as parties, judges, and lawyers in the legal domain; patients, doctors, and clinical staff in healthcare; and companies, investors, and regulators in finance---as well as the reliance on heterogeneous information sources. In the legal domain, these sources include court documents, such as civil court judgments, notary documents, or other contracts~\cite{HAN2024121052legalasst,amato2008,buey2016}. In the context of criminal investigations, it might be necessary to analyze chat messages extracted from suspects' mobile phone~\cite{subbulakshmi2024forensic,pirzada2024framework,kaushik2022forensic,Sonakshi_whatsapp_group_2025},  bank statements~\cite{data_mining_money_laundering_2003}, emails, or posts on social media platforms~\cite{jarrett2021impact}. In healthcare, relevant sources consist of laboratory reports~\cite{lab_reports_2023}, clinical trials, electronic health records, medical imaging, and data from health monitoring devices~\cite{XIAO2024108577}, while financial professionals consult financial news, statements, and market data~\cite{nlp_finance_survey_2025}.

In the rest of this thesis, we focus on the Italian legal domain. Specifically, we examine civil trials and criminal investigations through two types of sources: civil court judgments and investigative chat logs.

Civil court judgments are formal acts issued by the judge at the conclusion of a civil proceeding, constituting the official expression of the court's decision. They typically identify the parties involved, outline the relevant facts and procedural background, and present the legal reasoning supporting the decision, followed by the operative part specifying its effects. Their structure and mandatory elements are established by the Codice di procedura civile~\cite[Art.~132]{CPC}, which requires, among other things, the indication of the court and the parties, the statement of facts, and the reasoning in fact and law.

Investigative chat logs consist of messages extracted from \acp{ima}, such as WhatsApp\footnote{https://www.whatsapp.com/}, from the mobile phones of suspects during criminal investigations. These logs may include textual conversations, exchanged multimedia files, and metadata~\cite{P_rez_2021}. In the forensic context, such data are carefully collected and analyzed to reconstruct events~\cite{P_rez_2021}, identify relationships among participants~\cite{Sonakshi_whatsapp_group_2025}, for gathering evidence in support of the preceeding~\cite{pirzada2024framework}.

However, applying \ac{nlp} techniques to the legal domain entails specific challenges. For instance, in tasks such as estimating the similarity between different legal proceedings, using general-domain methods for document similarity may incorrectly label two cases as similar, even when the law treats them very differently. For example, the theft of similar products can be judged under different laws depending on the value of the stolen items~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}, potentially leading to very different penalties.

The concept of similarity between legal cases also strongly depends on the outcome of the trials~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}, which may be overlooked by general-domain \ac{nlp} approaches.
Moreover, this domain is characterized by a peculiar language that follows implicit structure, which may however vary depending on the country, on the court, or on the professionist that is writing the document~\cite{SANSONE2022101967,breit_legal_permits_2020}. The complexity of the legal language is further reflected in the nature of legal documents themselves, which are often lengthy and heterogeneous~\cite{SANSONE2022101967}.

Other challenges include compliance with regulations, as described above, as well as the evolving nature of the legal framework. Statutes may change over time, meaning that cases previously considered similar may no longer be so under current legislation~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. In addition, legal documents are structured into multiple sections serving distinct purposes~\cite[Art.~132]{CPC}, and the relevance of each section depends on the analytical task~\cite{feng-etal-2024-legal}. For example, when identifying similar cases, the comparison should focus on the factual and legal reasoning sections, which contain the elements that determine the applicability of legal provisions. In contrast, when searching for precedents involving the same individuals or organizations, the relevant information may appear primarily in the introductory sections describing the parties. Models that ignore this internal structure and treat the text as a single unstructured block risk misleading results—for instance, ranking two cases as similar simply because they mention the same persons, even though the underlying legal issues and outcomes differ substantially.

\paragraph{Main challenges addressed in this work}
In this work, we address several key challenges that arise when integrating \ac{ai}-based systems in knowledge-intensive and legally relevant domains:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textit{Ch.~\arabic*:},ref=\textit{Ch.~\arabic*}]
    \item \label{ch:scalability} \emph{Scalability.} Legal and investigative datasets may comprise thousands of heterogeneous documents, requiring solutions that can efficiently process and integrate large volumes of text and metadata.
    \item \label{ch:heterogeneity} \emph{Heterogeneity.} The documents vary widely in structure and type---ranging from judgments and contracts to chat logs and structured records---which demands flexible models capable of representing diverse sources within a unified framework.
    \item \label{ch:traceability} \emph{Traceability and verifiability.} Users must be able to verify the outcome of \ac{ai}-driven operations by tracing each piece of extracted or inferred information back to its source document~\cite[Art.~15]{ai_act}. Furthermore, without such mechanisms, user trust and accountability in \ac{ai}-assisted decision-making may be compromised~\cite{HAN2024121052legalasst}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Objectives}
\todo{central objective is too much. reduce to a mere objective like others}
Building on these challenges, the central objective of this \phd thesis is to design an architecture for data integration that addresses the \acl{ia} needs of professionals in the legal domain, supporting both formal legal documents---such as judgments and acts---and the heterogeneous data produced in investigative contexts.  
The proposed architecture is guided by the following principles:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generalizability} to heterogeneous documents and new use cases, as certain investigations may require ad-hoc workflows.
    \item \textbf{Traceability and Verifiability}, ensuring that the origin of any extracted information can always be verified. For instance, a user searching for documents mentioning a person must be able to read the exact mention within its context, while a \acl{qa} system should provide references to the text passages supporting its answers.
    \item \textbf{Error correction capability}, allowing users to identify and correct extraction or linking errors, thus improving system quality with a \acf{hitl}~\cite{klie-etal-2020-zero} validation approach.
    \item \textbf{Scalability} to large and continuously growing document collections.
\end{enumerate}

Additionally, it should be capable of integrating both existing knowledge from public repositories---such as entities from Wikipedia, Wikidata, or legal codes---and knowledge from \emph{novel entities}, not publicly available, such as the parties in the judgments or the people composing the suspect's communication network in an investigation. In the reamaining of this thesis, we will refer to this challenge as the \nilchallenge.

\paragraph{Research objectives and contributions}
Building on these design principles, this thesis pursues three main research objectives:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textit{Obj.~\arabic*:},ref=\textit{Obj.~\arabic*}]
    \item \label{ob:extraction_assessment} \textbf{Evaluation of the feasibility of existing \ac{ie} technologies in the legal domain.}  
    This involves experiments with \acl{ie} models (including \acl{ner}, \acl{nel}, and NIL prediction, that are described in \Cref{sec:information_extraction}) to assess their adaptability and performance on domain-specific data.

    \item \label{ob:architecture} \textbf{Design of an entity-centric architecture for data integration and \ac{ia}.}  
    The architecture organizes heterogeneous and unstructured information around entities (e.g., persons, locations, organizations, laws), supporting both legal and investigative sources.
    
    \item \label{ob:refactx} \textbf{Development of an efficient question answering system for providing grounded answers.}  
    The final contribution, \textbf{ReFactX}, is an efficient \acl{qa} system designed to provide answers grounded with explicit evidence from existing knowledge, enabling users to trace and verify given answers.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Design choices to achieve the objectives}
To meet these objectives, the proposed approach adopts an \emph{entity-centric} paradigm.  
Entities serve as conceptual anchors that connect information within and across documents, enabling cross-references and semantic aggregation.  
They also enhance generalizability, as entities are common across domains: examples include persons, judges, and legal acts in law; patients, diseases, and treatments in healthcare; or companies, investors, and assets in finance.  
As illustrated in \Cref{fig:entities_central}, entities act as the connective tissue linking diverse data types---ranging from structured bank transfers to unstructured reports, voice messages, and chat transcripts---enabling aggregation operations, such as computing counts or groupings over certain entities or entity types, and faceting, that is, organizing retrieved items along multiple dimensions to support interactive filtering and exploration~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted}. In this context, faceted search allows users to filter items---such as legal documents or chat messages---mentioning specific locations, persons, or laws, as well as to compose more complex queries combining multiple facets, for instance a location, a date, and certain persons.
