\begin{definition}[Question Answering]
    \emph{\Acf{qa}} refers to the task of producing a concise and relevant answer to a question expressed in natural language~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog}.
\end{definition}
\Ac{qa} systems may be focused on a specific domain, \eg supporting only questions regarding law---in this case we speak of \emph{closed-domain \acl{qa}}, or designed to support broad domain-free questions, \eg questions referring to large general-purpose \ac{kr}, such as Wikipedia~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}. As a consequence, open-domain \ac{qa} systems tend to more generalizable~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}, thus, in this thesis we concentrate on open-domain \ac{qa}.

These system must acquire external information to answer the given question and this information is generally acquired from unstructured textual document collections, structured \aclp{kb}, or a combination of both~\cite{caballero2021brief}. Modern \acp{llm} contain considerable factual knowledge in the parameters that allows them to answer general domain questions~\cite{chang_how_do_llm_acquire_factual_knowledge_2024,physics_of_lm_3.1_2024,petroni-etal-2019-language-models-kbs,roberts-etal-2020-how-much-kg-in-llm}, but information is constantly evolving and updating \ac{llm} knowledge generally requires expensive fine-tuning~\cite{kg_editing_survey_2024} or introduces the risk of side effects~\cite{li2024unveiling,cohen-etal-2024-rippleeffects}.


Two main paradigms exist for injecting external knowledge. The first is \emph{input-based knowledge injection},
where the external knowledge is provided in the model's input---either as plain text or dense vectors---together with the question.

This method has been referred to as \emph{retriever-reader}, where---given a question---the retriever retrieves relevant documents and pass them with the original question to the reader, which gives the final answer~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. Essentially, once relevant documents or passages are obtained, the open \ac{qa} tasks is reduced to a machine reading comprehension task, in which the reader's comprehension of the input document is tested with the question~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}.
For the retriever, both sparse and dense methods have been used~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. Sparse retrievers use traditional \ac{ir} techniques, such as TF-IDF or BM25~\cite{jm3}, that use sparse representations for query-document matching~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. On the other hand, dense retrievers use dense semantic representations~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}, but generally require encoder models, such as BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}, fine-tuned for maximizing the similarity (\eg dot-product) between the vectors of the question and the relevant text passages~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense}.

Also the more recent \acf{rag} paradigm~\cite{gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage,karpukhin-etal-2020-dense,lewis2020retrievalaugmented} belongs to this class of input-based methods. Indeed \ac{rag}---at least with the simplest naive implementation---retrieves relevant passages from a document collection and then passes them---as text~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense} or dense vectors~\cite{lewis2020retrievalaugmented}---as input to a generative models that generates the final answer. Essentially, this can be considered as the retriever-reader paradigm applied to \acp{llm}, with the advantage that these models can act as readers directly with \acl{icl}, without fine-tuning~\cite{gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage}. More advanced proposals to improve the \ac{rag} pipeline include the addition of pre-retrieval and post-retrieval operations and by involving \acp{llm} more \say{actively}, inspired by techniques for improve \acp{llm} reasoning, such as \acl{cot} prompting~\cite{wei2022chainofthought}. For example, \textcite{shao-etal-2023-enhancing} exploited the model generation for subsequent retrieve-generate iterations, while \textcite{asai2023selfrag} instructed a model to perform retrieval on-demand and to \say{reflect} on retrieved passages.

Following the same direction, agentic \acp{llm}~\cite{xi2025rise,plaat2025agenticlargelanguagemodels,yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting,NEURIPS2023_d842425e,thoppilan2022lamdalanguagemodelsdialog,lehmann2024beyond}---which perform actions, such as calling retrieval tools, and receive \say{observations}~\cite{yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting}---can be considered as a continuation of this trend that increases the involvement of \acp{llm} in actively retrieve external knowledge when needed.
Same as \ac{rag}, agentic methods insert the external knowledge, resulting from tools' output, into the model input. 

In general, input-based methods help mitigate hallucination~\cite{shuster-etal-2021-retrieval-augmentation}, but they rely on external retrieval components, which increases latency and system complexity, and inflates the number of input tokens~\cite{yang2024memory3,sun2025zerosearchincentivizesearchcapability}. Moreover, pipeline- or service-based designs require tuning a larger number of components and hinder effective back-propagation of supervision to early stages~\cite{zhou2025openragoptimizingragendtoend}. Such architectures are vulnerable to error propagation~\cite{le-fokkens-2017-tackling}, and propagating knowledge updates across all submodules (\eg QA and entity-linking KBs) can be non-trivial.


\medskip
\noindent

Alternatively, in \emph{latent-interaction knowledge injection} the external knowledge is incorporated \emph{within} the model architecture rather than through the input. A prominent family of approaches relies on non-parametric---\ie external to model parameters---memory modules integrated via cross-attention or learned keys~\cite{wu-etal-2022-efficient,yang2024memory3,deJong2022MentionMemory,NEURIPS2022_882d801f,pmlr-v162-borgeaud22a}. These architectures integrate knowledge more tightly but require architectural modifications, thus reducing the plug-and-play applicability of pretrained \acp{llm} in \ac{icl} settings.
\emph{Fusion-in-Decoder}~\cite{izacard-grave-2021-leveraging} can also be regarded as a latent-interaction method: in encoder--decoder architectures, the information from multiple retrieved passages is first encoded and then injected into the decoder. However, since it still depends on an external retriever and concatenates all encoded passages before decoding, it is less distant from input-based methods than architectures with explicit memory modules.


\paragraph{Knowledge Graph Question Answering}


When considering \acl{kg} as the source of external knowledge instead of document collections, we speak of \emph{\acf{kgqa}}: the answer is derived from \ac{kg} facts rather than from unstructured text~\cite{lan2022complex}. Two main lines exist~\cite{lan2022complex}. A preliminary step shared by both paradigms is to identify a topic entity by performing \acl{nel}. In the \emph{semantic-parsing} paradigm, the question is translated into an executable logical form (e.g.\ SPARQL~\cite{prudhommeaux2008sparql}) which is then executed over the \ac{kg}. These methods are interpretable but depend on parser quality~\cite{lan2022complex} and typically require question--SPARQL supervision~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn,li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs}. Common implementations include: ranking-based systems that enumerate candidate queries (from templates or by traversing the \ac{kg}) and score them with hand-crafted features or BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}; coarse-to-fine methods that predict a query skeleton and then ground it via \ac{nel}; and transformer-based generation~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017} also coupled with constrained decoding to ensure well-formed queries~\cite{kbqa_sem_parsing_2022}.

Conversely, \acl{ir}-based \ac{kgqa} better supports weak supervision, training only on question--answer pairs~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn}. These methods retrieve a question-specific subgraph and then rank or generate answers from it~\cite{lan2022complex}. Typically, the question and candidate answers (\ac{kg} nodes) are encoded into dense representations and compared by semantic similarity. Early work used neural encoders with hand-crafted features; more recent work leverages \ac{kg}-embeddings, graph-attention~\cite{velickovic2018graph}, or \acp{gnn}~\cite{ZHOU202057}. Many systems operate in a pipeline composed of entity extraction, generation of candidate paths (often restricted to 1--2 hops), and neural ranking or filtering~\cite{ZHANG2022102933,lan2022complex}.

More recently, \acp{llm} have been applied to \ac{kgqa}~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. Unlike text-based \ac{rag}, which feeds retrieved passages directly to the generator, graph-based \ac{kgqa} first retrieves a relevant subgraph and then converts it into an \ac{llm}-compatible representation before generation~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. The retrieval phase can be implemented with graph search algorithms, for example by identifying topic entities via \acl{nel} and expanding their neighborhoods~\cite{lan2022complex}. Other approaches use \acp{gnn} to encode graph data and then score candidate subgraphs by similarity to the query~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. For instance, GNN-RAG~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn} encodes the graph, assigns scores to nodes, and retrieves those above a threshold. \acp{lm} have been used to derive question-specific subgraphs, \eg by iteratively selecting relations adjacent to the question entities~\cite{zhang-etal-2022-subgraph,kim-etal-2023-kg}.

For answer generation, \ac{kgqa} systems either employ \acp{gnn} or encoder-only \acp{lm} to perform discriminative answer selection (\eg for multiple-choice questions), or otherwise verbalize the retrieved subgraph and feed it to a \ac{clm} for generation~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. Verbalization strategies range from triple templates (\eg ``subject -> predicate -> object''~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn}) to code-like or markup-based formats~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. All these methods correspond to input-based knowledge injection methods.

\paragraph{Constrained generation for \ac{qa}}

Constrained generation~\cite{cao2021autoregressive,scholak-etal-2021-picard}---which is described in \Cref{sec:constrained-gen} for generating \ac{kg} facts for \acl{kgqa}---offers a promising alternative to input- and memory-based knowledge integration by restricting the output space of \acp{llm} during decoding to sequences that satisfy structural, syntactic, or semantic constraints. It has been successfully applied to code generation~\cite{scholak-etal-2021-picard}, entity linking~\cite{cao2021autoregressive}, information retrieval~\cite{bevilacqua2022autoregressive}, and more recently to instruction-following tasks such as entity disambiguation~\cite{geng-etal-2023-grammar}. Two recent works have extended constrained generation to \ac{qa}~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs,luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}, guiding \acp{llm} through \ac{kg} paths during decoding. In particular, DoG~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs} alternates constrained triple generation with free-form reasoning in a \ac{kg}-grounded \ac{cot}-style process, while GCR~\cite{luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning} produces full \ac{kg} paths with a fine-tuned model and delegates the final answer to a larger \ac{llm}. However, both still depend on external entity linking and do not address scalability to very large knowledge bases, which remains a key open challenge for advancing constrained generation in \ac{kgqa}.
