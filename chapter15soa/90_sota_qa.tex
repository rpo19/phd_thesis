\section{Question Answering with External Knowledge}
\label{sec:sota:qa}

% from refact dog gcr
\begin{definition}[Question Answering]
    \emph{\Acf{qa}} refers to the task of producing a concise and relevant answer to a question expressed in natural language~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog}.
\end{definition}
\Ac{qa} systems may be focused on a specific domain, \eg supporting only questions regarding law---in this case we speak of \emph{closed-domain \acl{qa}}, or designed to support broad domain-free questions, \eg questions referring to large general-purpose \ac{kr}, such as Wikipedia~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}. As a consequence, open-domain \ac{qa} systems tend to be more generalizable~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}, thus, in this thesis we concentrate on open-domain \ac{qa}.

These systems must acquire external information to answer the given question and this information is generally acquired from unstructured textual document collections, structured \aclp{kb}, or a combination of both~\cite{caballero2021brief}. Modern \acp{llm} contain considerable factual knowledge in the parameters that allows them to answer general domain questions~\cite{chang_how_do_llm_acquire_factual_knowledge_2024,physics_of_lm_3.1_2024,petroni-etal-2019-language-models-kbs,roberts-etal-2020-how-much-kg-in-llm}, but information is constantly evolving and updating \ac{llm} knowledge generally requires expensive fine-tuning~\cite{kg_editing_survey_2024} or introduces the risk of side effects~\cite{li2024unveiling,cohen-etal-2024-rippleeffects}.


% \medskip
Two main paradigms exist for allowing \acp{llm} to acquire external knowledge: \emph{input-based} and \emph{memory-based} integration.

\paragraph{Input-based knowledge injection.}
Here, the external knowledge is concatenated with the question in the model input, either as plain text or dense vectors. This paradigm includes the traditional \emph{retriever–reader} framework, in which a retriever identifies relevant passages and a reader produces the final answer~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. Sparse retrievers rely on classical \ac{ir} models such as TF–IDF or BM25~\cite{jm3}, while dense retrievers use semantic encoders (e.g.\ BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}) fine-tuned to maximize question–passage similarity~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense}.

Sparse retrievers use traditional \ac{ir} techniques, such as TF-IDF or BM25~\cite{jm3}, that use sparse representations for query-document matching~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. On the other hand, dense retrievers use dense semantic representations~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey} of question and text passages, and generally require encoder models, such as BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}, fine-tuned for maximizing the similarity (\eg dot-product) between the vectors of a question and the relevant passages~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense}.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (\ac{rag})~\cite{lewis2020retrievalaugmented,gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage} applies this principle to generative models: retrieved passages are appended to the question and fed to an \ac{llm} that produces the answer, without fine-tuning, via \acl{icl}. Subsequent works enhance this pipeline through iterative retrieve–generate cycles~\cite{shao-etal-2023-enhancing} or self-directed retrieval~\cite{asai2023selfrag}.  
Agentic \acp{llm}~\cite{xi2025rise,yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting,plaat2025agenticlargelanguagemodels} continue this trend by dynamically invoking retrieval or reasoning tools, then incorporating their results back into the model input.

Indeed \ac{rag}---at least with the naive implementation---retrieves relevant passages from a document collection and then passes them---as text~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense} or dense vectors~\cite{lewis2020retrievalaugmented}---as input to a generative model that produces the final answer. Essentially, this can be considered as the retriever-reader paradigm applied to \acp{llm}, with the advantage that these models can act as readers directly with \acl{icl}, without fine-tuning~\cite{gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage}. More advanced proposals to enhance the \ac{rag} pipeline include the addition of pre-retrieval and post-retrieval operations and by involving \acp{llm} more \say{actively}, inspired by techniques for improving \acp{llm} reasoning, such as \acl{cot} prompting~\cite{wei2022chainofthought}. For example, \textcite{shao-etal-2023-enhancing} perform iterative retrieve--generate cycles, in which the context of each retrieval step is enriched by the context produced in previous iterations, while \textcite{asai2023selfrag} instructs a model to perform retrieval on-demand and to \say{reflect} on retrieved passages.

While these methods reduce hallucination~\cite{shuster-etal-2021-retrieval-augmentation}, they introduce latency,  architectural complexity as multiple components must be tuned and maintained, and inflates the number of input tokens~\cite{yang2024memory3,zhou2025openragoptimizingragendtoend}. Moreover, errors can propagate across pipeline stages~\cite{le-fokkens-2017-tackling}, and synchronizing knowledge updates among modules (e.g.\ \ac{qa} and entity-linking \acp{kb}) can be non-trivial.

Moreover, pipeline- or service-based designs require tuning a larger number of components and hinder effective back-propagation of supervision to early stages~\cite{zhou2025openragoptimizingragendtoend}. Such architectures are vulnerable to error propagation~\cite{le-fokkens-2017-tackling}, and propagating knowledge updates across all submodules (\eg QA and entity-linking KBs) can be non-trivial.


\paragraph{Memory-based knowledge injection.}
These methods integrate external information within the model architecture, typically through non-parametric memory modules accessed via cross-attention or learned keys~\cite{wu-etal-2022-efficient,yang2024memory3,deJong2022MentionMemory,pmlr-v162-borgeaud22a}. Although such integration allows tighter coupling between retrieval and reasoning, it requires architectural modifications, thus reducing plug-and-play compatibility with pretrained \acp{llm}.  
\emph{Fusion-in-Decoder}~\cite{izacard-grave-2021-leveraging} lies between the two paradigms: it encodes each retrieved passage separately and feeds all encoded representations to the transformer decoder, fusing them during generation but still depending on an external retriever.



% Alternatively, in \emph{latent-interaction knowledge injection} the external knowledge is incorporated \emph{within} the model architecture rather than through the input. A prominent family of approaches relies on non-parametric (external to model parameters) memory modules integrated via cross-attention or learned keys~\cite{wu-etal-2022-efficient,yang2024memory3,deJong2022MentionMemory,NEURIPS2022_882d801f,pmlr-v162-borgeaud22a}. These architectures integrate knowledge more tightly but require architectural modifications, thus reducing the plug-and-play applicability of pretrained \acp{llm} and \ac{icl}.
% \emph{Fusion-in-Decoder}~\cite{izacard-grave-2021-leveraging} can also be regarded as a latent-interaction method: in encoder--decoder architectures, the information from multiple retrieved passages is first encoded and then injected into the decoder. However, since it still depends on an external retriever and concatenates all encoded passages before decoding, it is less distant from input-based methods than architectures with explicit memory modules.



\paragraph{Knowledge Graph Question Answering.}
When the external source is a \acl{kg}, the task becomes \emph{\acf{kgqa}}, where answers are derived from factual triples instead of unstructured text~\cite{lan2022complex}. Two main paradigms exist~\cite{lan2022complex}:  
(i)~\emph{Semantic-parsing} methods translate the question into an executable logical form (e.g.\ SPARQL~\cite{prudhommeaux2008sparql}), which is run on the \ac{kg}. These methods are interpretable but depend on parser accuracy and require costly annotations of question–query pairs~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs,mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn}.  
(ii)~\emph{Information-retrieval-based} methods operate with weaker supervision, learning from question–answer pairs. They extract a question-specific subgraph and then rank or generate answers from it using dense encoders, \ac{kg} embeddings, or \acp{gnn}~\cite{lan2022complex,ZHANG2022102933}. Most implementations are pipeline-based, combining entity extraction, graph traversal (typically 1–2 hops), and neural ranking.

Typically, the question and candidate answers (\ac{kg} nodes) are encoded into dense representations and compared by semantic similarity. \Acp{llm} have recently been integrated into \ac{kgqa}~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}, where the retrieved subgraph is converted into an \ac{llm}-compatible format and then verbalized for generation. In these methods, the \ac{kg} subgraph is first converted into a textual form---typically as verbalized triples (\eg ``subject → predicate → object'')~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn} or markup-style encodings~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}---and then provided to the model as input. Despite their effectiveness, they still rely on explicit retrieval and verbalization, thus remaining input-based.

\paragraph{Constrained Generation for QA.}
Constrained generation~\cite{cao2021autoregressive,scholak-etal-2021-picard} restricts the decoding process of \acp{llm} to sequences satisfying predefined syntactic or semantic constraints, thus combining generative flexibility with formal control. It has been successfully applied to tasks such as code generation~\cite{scholak-etal-2021-picard}, entity linking~\cite{cao2021autoregressive}, and information retrieval~\cite{bevilacqua2022autoregressive}.  
Recent extensions to \ac{qa} guide \acp{llm} through \ac{kg} paths during decoding~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs,luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}. For instance, DoG~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs} alternates constrained triple generation with free-form reasoning in a \ac{kg}-grounded \acl{cot} process, while GCR~\cite{luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning} produces full \ac{kg} paths and delegates final answering to a larger model.  

Yet, they still rely on external \acl{nel} modules and cannot operate efficiently on very large \acp{kb}. \Cref{chap:refactx} addresses this limitation by enabling direct constrained generation on large knowledge bases, without retrievers or pipelines.
