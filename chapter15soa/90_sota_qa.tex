\begin{definition}[Question Answering]
    \emph{\Acf{qa}} refers to the task of producing a concise and relevant answer to a question expressed in natural language~\cite{entityorientedsearch2018balog}.
\end{definition}
\Ac{qa} systems may be focused on a specific domain, \eg supporting only questions regarding law---in this cas we speak of \emph{closed-domain \acl{qa}}, or designed to support broad domain-free questions, \eg questions referring to large general-purpose \ac{kr}, such as Wikipedia~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}. As a consequence, open-domain \ac{qa} systems tend to more generalizable~\cite{zaib2022conversationalqasurvey}, thus, in this thesis we concentrate on open-domain \ac{qa}.

These system must acquire external information to answer the given question and this information is generally acquired from unstructured textual document collections, structured \aclp{kb}, or a combination of both~\cite{caballero2021brief}. Modern \acp{llm} contain considerable factual knowledge in the parameters that allows them to answer general domain questions~\cite{chang_how_do_llm_acquire_factual_knowledge_2024,physics_of_lm_3.1_2024,petroni-etal-2019-language-models-kbs,roberts-etal-2020-how-much-kg-in-llm}, but information is constantly evolving and updating \ac{llm} knowledge generally requires expensive fine-tuning~\cite{kg_editing_survey_2024} or introduces the risk of side effects~\cite{li2024unveiling,cohen-etal-2024-rippleeffects}.


Two main paradigms exist for injecting external knowledge. The first is \emph{input-based KE-QA},
where the external knowledge is provided in the model's input---either as plain text or dense vectors---together with the question.

This method has been referred to as \emph{retriever-reader}, where---given a question---the retriever retrieves relevant documents and pass them with the original question to the reader, which gives the final answer~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. Essentially, once relevant documents or passages are obtained, the open \ac{qa} tasks is reduced to a machine reading comprehension task, in which the reader's comprehension of the input document is tested with the question~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}.
For the retriever, both sparse and dense methods have been used~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. Sparse retrievers use traditional \ac{ir} techniques, such as TF-IDF or BM25~\cite{jm3}, that use sparse representations for query-document matching~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}. On the other hand, dense retrievers use dense semantic representations~\cite{zhu2021retrievingreadingcomprehensivesurvey}, but generally require encoder models, such as BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}, fine-tuned for maximizing the similarity (\eg dot-product) between the vectors of the question and the relevant text passages~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense}.

Also the more recent \acf{rag} paradigm~\cite{gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage,karpukhin-etal-2020-dense,lewis2020retrievalaugmented} belongs to this class of input-based methods. Indeed \ac{rag}---at least with the simplest naive implementation---retrieves relevant passages from a document collection and then passes them---as text~\cite{karpukhin-etal-2020-dense} or dense vectors~\cite{lewis2020retrievalaugmented}---as input to a generative models that generates the final answer. Essentially, this can be considered as the retriever-reader paradigm applied to \acp{llm}, with the advantage that these models can act as readers directly with \acl{icl}, without fine-tuning~\cite{gao2024retrievalaugmentedgenerationlargelanguage}. More advanced proposals to improve the \ac{rag} pipeline include the addition of pre-retrieval and post-retrieval operations and by involving \acp{llm} more \say{actively}, inspired by techniques for improve \acp{llm} reasoning, such as \acl{cot} prompting~\cite{wei2022chainofthought}. For example, \textcite{shao-etal-2023-enhancing} exploited the model generation for subsequent retrieve-generate iterations, while \textcite{asai2023selfrag} instructed a model to perform retrieval on-demand and to \say{reflect} on retrieved passages.

Following the same direction, agentic \acp{llm}~\cite{xi2025rise,plaat2025agenticlargelanguagemodels,yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting,NEURIPS2023_d842425e}---which perform actions, such as calling retrieval tools, and receive \say{observations}~\cite{yao2023react}---can be considered as a continuation of this trend that increases the involvement of \acp{llm} in actively retrieve external knowledge when needed.
Same as \ac{rag}, agentic methods insert the external knowledge, resulting from tools' output, into the model input. 

In general, input-based methods are able to reduce hallucination~\cite{shuster-etal-2021-retrieval-augmentation} but increase latency,  complexity, and inflate the number of input tokens~\cite{yang2024memory3,sun2025zerosearchincentivizesearchcapability}.

\paragraph{KGQA as a subcase of knowledge-enhanced QA.}


When considering \acl{kg} as the source of external knowledge instead of document collections, we speak of \emph{\acf{kgqa}}: the answer is derived from \ac{kg} facts rather than from unstructured text~\cite{lan2022complex}. Two main lines exist~\cite{lan2022complex}. A preliminary step shared by both paradigms is to identify a topic entity by performing \acl{nel}. In the \emph{semantic-parsing} paradigm, the question is translated into an executable logical form (e.g.\ SPARQL~\cite{prudhommeaux2008sparql}) which is then executed over the \ac{kg}. These methods are interpretable but depend on parser quality~\cite{lan2022complex} and typically require question--SPARQL supervision~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn,li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs}. Common implementations include: ranking-based systems that enumerate candidate queries (from templates or by traversing the \ac{kg}) and score them with hand-crafted features or BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}; coarse-to-fine methods that predict a query skeleton and then ground it via \ac{nel}; and transformer-based generation~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017} also coupled with constrained decoding to ensure well-formed queries~\cite{kbqa_sem_parsing_2022}.

Conversely, \acl{ir}-based \ac{kgqa} better supports weak supervision, training only on question--answer pairs~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn}. These methods retrieve a question-specific subgraph and then rank or generate answers from it~\cite{lan2022complex}. Typically, the question and candidate answers (\ack{kg} nodes) are encoded into dense representations and compared by semantic similarity. Early work used neural encoders with hand-crafted features; more recent work leverages \ac{kg}-embeddings, graph-attention~\cite{velickovic2018graph}, or \acp{gnn}~\cite{ZHOU202057}. Many systems operate in a pipeline composed of entity extraction, generation of candidate paths (often restricted to 1--2 hops), and neural ranking or filtering~\cite{ZHANG2022102933,lan2022complex}.



\paragraph{LLMs for KGQA}

More recently, \acp{llm} have been applied to \ac{kgqa}~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}.
Unlike text-based RAG, which feeds retrieved passages directly to the generator, graph-based \ac{kgqa} first retrieves a relevant subgraph and then converts it into an LLM-compatible representation before generation~\cite{peng2024graphretrievalaugmentedgenerationsurvey}. The retrieval phase can be handled with graph search algorithms, by identifying topic entities with \acl{nel} and consider their neiborhood~\cite{lan2022complex}. Other approaches use \acp{gnn} by encode graph data and subsequently score different
retrieval granularities based on their similarity to the query. For example, GNN-RAG~\cite{mavromatis-karypis-2025-gnn} first
encodes the graph, assigns a score to each entity, and retrieves entities relevant to the query based
on a threshold. Encoder-based \acp{lm} have been used also in the retrieval phase, \eg by iteratively selecting relations to expand paths starting from a topic entity~\cite{zhang-etal-2022-subgraph}. Also \acp{clm} have been used for building question subgraphs, \eg to map sentences to relations of question entities~\cite{kim-etal-2023-kg}.

Knowledge Graph QA (KGQA) historically pursued access to external knowledge through \emph{executable
queries} on a graph store. Semantic-parsing approaches~\cite{Lan_Jiang_2020,Ye_2022,Yu_2023} translate questions
into logical queries (e.g.\ SPARQL) and execute them against the KG. This can be viewed as a
KE-QA instance where KG access is \emph{delegated to a query compiler} rather than retrieved textually.
However, such pipelines require gold logical forms for training and may generate non-executable queries.

More recently, LLM-based KGQA~\cite{Salnikov_2023,Wu_2023,He_2024} has aligned with the two KE-QA paradigms above:
(i) input-based KGQA, where KG facts are first retrieved (explicitly or via learned retrievers) and fed to
an LLM~\cite{Jin_2024,Sun_2024b,Ren_2024,Luo_2024,Mavromatis_Karypis_2024,Zhuang_2024}; and (ii) interaction-based KGQA, where LLMs
are prompted to traverse the KG step by step (e.g.\ ToG~\cite{Sun_2024a} or Tree-of-Traversals~\cite{Markowitz_2024}),
thus using the KG as an external structured search space. Both classes ultimately turn KG knowledge
into textual input to an LLM, thus inheriting input-growth and latency issues.

\paragraph{Constrained generation for KE-QA.}
A third, more recent direction incorporates external knowledge not by feeding or storing it, but by
\emph{constraining the search space of generation}~\cite{cao2021autoregressive,scholak-etal-2021-picard,bevilacqua2022autoregressive,geng-etal-2023-grammar}.
Constrained decoding enforces that output tokens conform to a target grammar or vocabulary space,
without injecting external text. When applied to KE-QA with KGs~\cite{li2024decodinggraphsfaithfulsound,luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning},
the model is guided to generate KG-consistent triples or paths, grounding reasoning in the graph while
avoiding explicit retrieval. Existing constrained KE-QA approaches, however, either assume access to
small KGs or still depend on entity linking for grounding, thus retaining some limitations of input-based
pipelines. Our work positions itself in this constrained generation line, using constrained decoding
directly over large-scale KG facts (Wikidata-scale) without relying on retrieval or architectural changes.
