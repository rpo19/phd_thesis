Several systems for assisting legal professionals have been developed over time~\cite{amato2008,buey2016,breit_legal_permits_2020,HAN2024121052legalasst,human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}.
Early work by \textcite{amato2008} introduced a document management system for the legal domain that leverages ontologies and entity extraction, focusing primarily on notary documents.
\textcite{buey2016} focused on a wider range of legal documents---such as public acts and private agreements---by employing ontologies that model document structures and specify which entities should be extracted from each section.
\textcite{breit_legal_permits_2020} later explored the automation of processes related to legal permits\todo{add description once verified}.
More recent work include \textcite{HAN2024121052legalasst,human-ai-system-legal-precedent-search-china}. The former proposed \emph{LegalAsst}, a system designed to enhance court productivity by integrating \acp{llm}, entity recognition, graph-based visualizations of trial facts, and decision-tree representations of judicial reasoning.
The latter developed a prototype for legal case retrieval that guides professionals interactively through a faceted search interface---an exploratory search paradigm that organizes results along multiple dimensions, or facets~\cite{ARMENTANO2014_nlp_faceted,hearst_2006_faceted}, such as case type, court, date, or involved parties---recommending alternative search terms to support precedent discovery.

While these work devlop ontologies or multiple services including extraction and visualization tools, they do not focus much the architecture design or implementation.

Eunomos~\cite{boella2016eunomos} is a legal data management system that integrates legislative XML --- used to represent statutes in a structured and versioned form --- with legal ontologies encoding legal concepts and relations (e.g., \emph{replaced-by} for amendments). The platform automatically acquires legislation (e.g., from Normattiva), converts it to XML, extracts cross-references to build a network of norms, and maintains temporal versions of provisions. Concepts, prescriptions, roles, and sanctions are stored in an ontology and linked to specific legislative fragments, enabling semantic navigation and fine-grained queries. Population is semi-automatic: the system proposes annotations while still allowing manual correction. Targeted use cases include compliance in financial regulation and support for law firms and public bodies in tracking and structuring legal knowledge, as well as citizen-facing access to consolidated and concept-aware legislation. By coupling structured legislation with a concept layer, Eunomos goes beyond keyword-based retrieval and enables controlled, ontology-driven access to evolving legal norms.

\textcite{boella2019semi} extend Eunomos with improved \ac{nlp} services to reduce the manual effort of knowledge population via semi-automatic classification of norms, concept and relation extraction, amendment extraction, and \acl{el} using pattern-matching rules. A web-based \acl{hitl} interface is provided for validating the \ac{el} outputs, and a similar \ac{ui} is envisioned for supporting semi-automatic refinement of amendment extraction.

\textcite{beniamino_strada_2022} acknowledge the importance of organizing the documentation attached to legal trials and propose a workflow for automatically determining the \emph{proposability} of the instances presented to Courts, with the goal of reducing the burden on judges. They build a combined ontology from proposability, juridical, and road-accident ontologies, and train an ad-hoc \ac{ner} model to recognize domain-specific entities in trial documents (e.g.\ \emph{injured party}, \emph{tax identification code}, \emph{description of the facts}, \emph{lawyer}, \emph{statement}). Proposability is then computed by logical inference over the extracted entities. This work shares similarities with some of the contributions of this \phd, since it adopts an entity-centric approach and employs \ac{ner} for knowledge extraction, as in \textcite{BELLANDI2024,aixia_2023,pozzi_wise_2025}. It also releases an annotated dataset for training \ac{ner}, as in \textcite{BELLANDI2024,pozzi_wise_2025}. In addition, the authors provide user-facing \acp{ui} for visualizing proposability analysis and for displaying \ac{ner} annotations highlighted on documents side-by-side with the ontology, similarly to the \acp{ui} presented in \textcite{BELLANDI2024,pozzi_wise_2025,Agazzi2025DAVE}. However, the work does not describe a general legal architecture; it rather specifies a workflow for extracting ontology concepts with \ac{ner} to support the specific proposability task.

\textcite{balansay2024development} focus on notarial documents and aim to reduce the time-consuming manual operations carried out by professionals for document management. They develop a management system with a database for storing documents, organizing them into folders, searching them, and sharing them with colleagues — all provided through a web-based \ac{ui}.

\textcite{enase22,icaart23,pham2025improving} focus on Vietnamese legislation and introduce the Legal-Onto model~\cite{enase22}, an ontology framework structured around concepts, relations, and inference rules. A conceptual graph links key phrases to legal concepts, supporting semantic representation and retrieval. In later work~\cite{pham2025improving}, the authors address the problem of keeping the ontology up to date by parsing and structuring legal documents, extracting key phrases, and reconstructing the conceptual graph, as well as comparing different versions of laws through vector-based similarity. The overall architecture consists of the legal database — where documents are parsed and segmented into sections, articles, clauses, etc. — and the conceptual graph built from them, along with user interfaces for search and exploration~\cite{icaart23,enase22}.


Lynx~\cite{MorenoSchneider2021LynxCuration,RodriguezDoncel2019LynxDMP} provides a microservice-based architecture for building and exploiting a multilingual Legal Knowledge Graph for compliance. Documents from heterogeneous legal sources are assigned URIs, enriched with metadata, decomposed into parts, and exposed through APIs. The platform offers services for search (including over annotations), question answering, terminology access, and workflow execution over documents via an orchestrator that coordinates document-level operations, in a spirit similar to the automation pipelines in \cite{BELLANDI2024}. A validation controller ensures integrity by enforcing that stored documents conform to the data model (text, metadata, identifier, type). The Legal Knowledge Graph aggregates and interlinks legal and regulatory data across jurisdictions and languages, leveraging Linked Data principles. Although the broader legal graph on the Web is unbounded, Lynx materializes and serves the core entities relevant for its services, primarily legal documents (acts, directives, standards), concept schemes, and selected organizational entities. Storage is implemented using a dual-layer document manager, combining Elasticsearch with a Trellis Linked Data Platform, using JSON-LD to guarantee interoperability and to store the Lynx Legal Knowledge Graph as RDF-native resources.

On the other hand, approaches supporting criminal investigations have targeted illicit Web data~\cite{kejriwal2017searchengine,kejriwal2018}, particularly in efforts to combat human trafficking. These works propose an entity-centric scalable architecture that constructs a \acl{kg}---according to a domain-specific ontology---and provides advanced \acl{ia} \acp{ui}, such as faceted search interfaces and map-based visualizations. Although the architecture could be generalized to other types of documents---with a different ontology---they did no experiment with multimedia data and implement the extraction services for specific challenges of illicit data, such as information that is often hidden in plain sight, such as phone numbers deliberately obfuscated by mixing digits and words.

% both focus also on scalability
\textcite{P_rez_2021} proposed a modular platform for analyzing vast and heterogeneous data---including multimedia content---from multiple sources. Their system is built upon an ontology composed by the entities \texttt{Person}, \texttt{Location}, \texttt{Organization}, \texttt{Equipment}, and \texttt{Event}, and adopts a scalable microservices-based architecture~\cite{microservices_2016} that integrates components such as speech-to-text and keyword extraction modules to populate a \ac{kb}. While the architecture is both modular and generalizable, their experiments rely primarily on keyword-based extraction techniques, which provide limited semantic understanding and require manual intervention.


\textcite{bellandi2025streamlining} present an evolution of the work in~\textcite{BELLANDI2024}, which constitutes one of the contributions of this \phd. The resulting IDJ platform enforces streamlined management and analysis of legal documents (e.g., court decisions, orders) to serve different legal actors through service pipelines. Its architecture is modular and aligns with the principles underlying \ref{ob:architecture}: a text-processing layer handles ingestion, storage (ElasticSearch for texts/metadata, SQL for annotations), and basic NLP pre-processing, while a dedicated NLP layer performs \ac{ner}, \ac{nel}, and concept extraction. A graph database is used as an entity registry, exposed through dedicated APIs. Pipelines are orchestrated through a Kafka-based mechanism and expose all services through standard APIs. The platform supports entity-based analytics (e.g., entity distributions, paths between entities across documents) and concept-based document exploration, enabling navigation, search, and statistics over the corpus using extracted entities and concepts. Front-end modules provide UIs for exploration and analytics, extending the interfaces in~\textcite{BELLANDI2024}. Overall, the work focuses on operationalizing document--annotation--entity principles through an extensible pipeline architecture, rather than proposing a standalone theoretical model.
