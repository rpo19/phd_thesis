% \subsection{\ac{nel} for Legal Documents}

Most prior work on \acl{ee} in the legal domain has primarily addressed the \acl{ner} component. Early \ac{ner} systems relied on handcrafted rules and statistical models~\cite{ccetindaug2023name_legal_turkish}, including CRFs~\cite{Lafferty2001}. Later work adopted BiLSTM$+$CRF architectures for Brazilian and German legal corpora~\cite{ccetindaug2023name_legal_turkish}. With the advent of transformers~\cite{vaswani_transformers_2017} and BERT~\cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}, domain-adapted models such as LEGAL-BERT were released~\cite{chalkidis-etal-2020-legal}. Subsequent comparisons~\cite{Keshavarz2022} reported that LEGAL-BERT achieves performance similar to simpler neural models (LSTMs, CNNs) on \ac{ner}.

Only a few works have addressed \ac{nel} on legal texts. One early study applied \ac{ner} and \ac{nel} to European Court of Human Rights decisions~\cite{cardellino_etal_2017_lowcost}, using a legal ontology aligned with YAGO~\cite{mahdisoltani2013yago3,yago2_2013,yago2s_demo_2013,yago_core_2007}. Another focused exclusively on \ac{nel} over EUR-Lex articles~\cite{Elnaggar2018} using transfer learning. Hybrid pipelines have also been explored: \textcite{Tamper2020} combine BERT-based and rule-based \ac{ner} with an off-the-shelf \ac{nel} service using popularity-based disambiguation for Finnish court decisions.

Some work on \ac{nel} with NIL prediction has been evaluated on historically legal material, such as the 1641 Irish rebellion depositions~\cite{klie-etal-2020-zero}. However, we are not aware of prior work that jointly studies \ac{ner}, \ac{nel}, and NIL prediction on recent legal corpora.

\subsection{Entity Extraction in the Italian Language}
\label{sec:sota:ee:ita}

Italian datasets for \ac{ner} include multilingual resources~\cite{tedeschi-navigli-2022-multinerd,NOTHMAN2013151} as well as domain-specific corpora, e.g.\ in the medical domain~\cite{CATELLI2020106779}. Italian \ac{nel} datasets likewise comprise multilingual resources such as VoxEL~\cite{rosales2018voxel} and collections based on short texts~\cite{basile2016neelit}.

Among ready-to-use libraries supporting Italian, \spacy~\cite{honnibal2020spacy} and Tint~\cite{2018tint2} provide \ac{ner}, while DBpedia Spotlight~\cite{mendes_2011_spotlight,Daiber2013spotlight} performs both \ac{ner} and \ac{nel}. \Spacy offers pre-trained \ac{ner} models of different sizes but currently includes no transformer-based model for Italian. Tint implements \ac{ner} using CRF taggers complemented by rule-based modules for dates and monetary amounts. 

With respect to \ac{nel}, DBpedia Spotlight is a ready-to-use tool that performs both \ac{ner} and \ac{nel} against DBpedia~\cite{mendes_2011_spotlight,Daiber2013spotlight} also in Italian\footnote{\url{https://github.com/dbpedia-spotlight/dbpedia-spotlight-model}}. However, it is based on earlier generation techniques and has been shown to require substantial improvement to remain competitive with more recent approaches~\cite{ficlone}.
