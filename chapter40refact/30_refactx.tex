\section{Constrained Fact-Generation}
\label{sec:constrained-gen}

The mechanism of constrained generation alters the autoregressive next-token generation process of causal language modeling (\acs{clm}). As formulated in 
\Cref{sec:rw:llms}, normally an \ac{llm}, parameterized by $\theta$, given an initial sequence of tokens $X = [x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{|X|}]$ and a vocabulary $V = \{v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_{|V|}\}$, estimates the probability distribution of $x_i$ over the entire $V$:
\begin{equation}
    P_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}) \qquad \forall v \in V.
\end{equation}
The next-token $x_i$ can be chosen according to different sampling strategy;
% when sampling is disabled
in greedy decoding the most probable token is chosen as follows:
%
\begin{equation}
    x_i = \text{argmax}_{v \in V}P_\theta(x_i = v\mid x_{<i})
    \label{eq:token_selection}
\end{equation}

Intuitively, to constrain this process for generating only existing facts, we need to restrict $V$ to only allow tokens that can form a fact from the \ac{kb}. We define $V^A_{X} \subseteq V$, which contains all the tokens that can lead to an existing fact if added to the sequence $X$. Considering the example in \Cref{fig:constrained_generation}, at step $x_i$, when $x_{<i}=\text{``<Danny Boyle> <''}$, $V^A_{x_{<i}} = \{\text{``date''}, \text{``given''}\}$.




Next, we define the \textit{next tokens} function $\mathrm{NT}_{\mathrm{kb}}$ that, given a sequence $x_{<i}$, obtains $V^A_{x_{<i}}$ for the considered \ac{kb}.
%
Consequently, the token selection formula from \Cref{eq:token_selection} is updated to:
\begin{equation}
    x_i = \text{argmax}_{v \in V^A_{x_{<i}}}P_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}), \qquad \text{where }
    V^A_{x_{<i}} = \mathrm{NT}_{\mathrm{kb}}(x_{<i}) = \{v_0,v_1,\ldots\}.
\end{equation}

However, the same result can be achieved by altering the probability distribution, setting the probability of all forbidden tokens to zero---without modifying the vocabulary:
\begin{equation}
    P^{c}_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) = \begin{cases}
        P_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}) & \forall v \in V^A_{x_{<i}}, \\
        0 & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
By using $P^{c}_\theta$, instead of $P_\theta$, we achieve constrained generation, relying on the assumption that the sampling strategy (\Cref{eq:token_selection}) would never choose any $t_{k+1} \mid P^c(t_{k+1}) = 0$.
%
In practice, since at implementation level the models use log-probabilities, we directly the log-probabilities of forbidden tokens to $-\infty$\footnote{Hugging Face Transformers LogitsProcessor is used to alter the log-probabilities.}:
\begin{equation}
    \log P^{c}_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) = \begin{cases}
        \log P_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}) & \forall v \in V^A_{x_{<i}}, \\
        -\infty & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\Cref{fig:constrained_generation} illustrates the application of constrained generation. It shows two facts produced by the same \ac{llm} in response to the question \textit{``When was Danny Boyle born?''}---first using normal (unconstrained) generation, highlighted in red, and then using constrained generation, underlined in blue.
While, the fact generated in normal mode is not correct, constrained generation is able to guide the \ac{llm} to the correct fact. The lower part of the figure details the mechanism of constrained generation. For each decoding step, allowed tokens are displayed in green boxes, whereas forbidden tokens are displayed in red boxes with dashed borders and a strike-through.
Tokens are arranged in ascending order according to $P_\theta(x_k = v)$, from bottom to top.

Starting from the sequence 
$x_{<i} = \text{``<Danny Boyle> <''}$, normal generation would generate
\begin{equation}
    x_i = \text{argmax}_{v \in V}P_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}) = \text{``born''},
\end{equation}
leading to an incorrect fact, while with constrained generation
\begin{equation}
    x_i = \text{argmax}_{v \in V}P^c_\theta(x_i = v \mid x_{<i}) = \text{``date''}.
\end{equation}
%
This happens because in the \ac{kb} there is no fact starting with \textit{``<Danny Boyle> <born''}.

Subsequently, when generating $x_{i+6}$, the constrained generation mechanism guides the model to select ``9'' that leads to the correct birth year of Danny Boyle \textit{``<Danny Boyle> <born> <1956''}, avoiding the model to generate \textit{``<Danny Boyle> <born> <16''} or \textit{``<Danny Boyle> <born> <196''}, both leading to incorrect information.
This mechanism is additionally improved by beam search~\cite{hu-etal-2015-improved} which allows the model to explore multiple paths in the prefix tree in parallel.

\section[Scaling to 800 Million Facts from Wikidata][Scaling to 800M Facts]{Scaling to 800 Million Facts from Wikidata}
\label{sec:wikidata-repr}

To demonstrate the scalability of \refactx (\ref{ch:scalability}), we use Wikidata~\cite{wikidata} as a large-scale \ac{kb}. While this study focuses on structured knowledge, applying \refactx in the legal domains remains an avenue for future work, as it would likely require to extract facts from text.

From the Wikidata truthy dump---which contains the highest-confidence statements while excluding qualifiers\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Wikidata:Database_download/en\#RDF\_dumps}}---we extract approximately 800 million triples\footnote{The dump from 11 December 2024 was used.}.
%
To filter out uninformative facts only triples whose subject and relation have Wikidata identifiers are retained. For the object, we allow entities with Wikidata identifiers, English literals, numbers, dates, and literals with no language.

Then, for each entity, we obtain a meaningful textual label---because Wikidata IDs lack meaning for \acp{llm}---corresponding to the Wikipedia title if the entity is described in English Wikipedia\footnote{\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/}}. Otherwise, since the Wikidata label alone is not unique, the entity description is additionally considered, using the following template.
\begin{verbatim}
    {entity label} ({entity description})
\end{verbatim}
For example, the entity \textsc{Jane Hajduk}\footnote{\url{https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q3734827}}, which is not present in English Wikipedia, is labeled as ``Jane Hajduk (American actress)''. In this case, the label comes from \texttt{rdfs:label}~\cite{rdfs-spec} and the description from \texttt{schema:description}~\cite{schemaorg-spec}.



With this process more than 800 million facts like the ones underlined in \Cref{fig:example} were obtained.


% Once the facts are tokenized and indexed in a prefix tree for efficient access.
Additionally, while creating the tree, we calculate the number of reachable leaves from each node to avoid that \acp{llm} generate the same fact repeatedly, a behavior witnessed during preliminary experiments.

Consider the prefix $X = \text{``<Euro> <country> <S''}$ in \Cref{fig:trie_example}, where the number or reachable leaves is $numleaves(X)=2$, duplicate facts generation is prevented as follows: after generating $\text{``<Euro> <country> <Slovakia> .''}$, the number of reachable leaves is decreased to $1$ and from $X$ the model is only allowed to select $\text{``lovenia> .''}$. At this point, all leaves reachable from $X$ have been generated, thus generating ``S'' from $X'=\text{``<Euro> <country> <''}$ is forbidden by constrained generation. From $X'$ the \ac{llm} can generate one of the remaining $numleaves(X') - 2 = 24$ facts (\eg $\text{``<Euro> <country> <Italy> .''}$). 

While relatively small trees can be kept in memory, \eg using Python dictionaries, when dealing with bigger trees, such as the 800-million-fact tree derived from Wikidata, this is often impossible. Therefore, we rely on relational database software. PostgreSQL 17.2~\cite{postgresql_online} has been chosen and the fact-tree table was designed to ensure that all the information required for token generation is efficiently available.
Given a prefix $X$, this includes the allowed next tokens $V^A_{X} = \{v_0,v_1,\ldots\}$, the number of leaves reachable from $X$, and the number of reachable leaves if choosing any $v \in V^A_{X}$ (to avoid selecting 0-leaves tokens and generating a fact twice). In \Cref{tab:postgres-table}, they are depicted respectively as \textit{Next Tokens}, \textit{\#Lv. (number of leaves)}, and \textit{Child.\#Lv.}.

However, this tree-representation schema can quickly grow in disk space usage, as a single path of length $L$ requires storing $L-1$ rows. To reduce the disk space requirements, two additional measures are applied. First, after a certain prefix length $L_c$ we stop adding rows and instead directly save the subtree in Pickle format\footnote{\url{https://docs.python.org/3/library/pickle.html}}, as shown in the last row of \Cref{tab:postgres-table}.
Consequently, at inference time subtrees are manageable in size and ca be directly loaded in memory. In our case, with $L_c = 7$, 99\% of the subtrees use less than 116 kilobytes of memory.
Secondly, single-leaf sequences are represented in a single row, as visible at the fourth row in \Cref{tab:postgres-table}, saving the rest of the sequence in \textit{Child.\#Lv.} (the token 29 comes after 694, and 662 is the last in the sequence).
%
List items are saved as PostgreSQL arrays, while Pickle data is saved in \texttt{BYTEA}. For fast access, the \textit{Prefix} is indexed with a B-Tree\footnote{\url{https://www.postgresql.org/docs/current/btree.html}} index that provides logarithmic search time~\cite{cormen2022introduction}.

The ingestion process consists of constructing the tree in memory and then persisting it to the database. However, hardware memory constraints prevent keeping the entire tree in memory at once. For this reason, ingestion is performed in batches: a limited-size tree is built, persisted, and then discarded before proceeding to the next batch.

This approach inevitably introduces duplicated prefix rows, since identical prefixes originating from different batches are stored as distinct rows and must be merged at inference time. In the worst case, the number of duplicates is bounded by the number of batches used. Despite this overhead, the proposed mechanisms allow indexing 800 million facts using approximately 95~GB of on-disk storage.

Note that the index stores token IDs and therefore depends on the vocabulary of the underlying \ac{llm}. As a consequence, \acp{llm} with different vocabularies require separate indexes.

% At the implementation level, the functions $\mathrm{NT}_{\mathrm{kb}}$ and $numleaves$ access the prefix tree to obtain, respectively, the set of allowed next tokens and the number of reachable leaves for a given prefix. This information is acquired either through a SQL query to the database or from the in-memory subtree, which is loaded after prefix length exceeds $L_c$.









\section[Embedding \refactx into Question-Answering Workflows][\refactx for QA]{Embedding \refactx into Question-Answering Workflows}
\label{sec:refactx-qa}

\refactx relies on In-Context Learning (ICL)~\cite{dong2024survey} for instructing the model to access external knowledge.
Our prompt, shown in \Cref{fig:prompt}, instructs the \ac{llm} to, first, determine the reasoning path needed for answering, then to use the \emph{Fact} command for getting relevant facts from the \ac{kb}, and finally to answer based on the proofs acquired with the \emph{Fact} command. Additionally, we instruct the model to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item determine the required answer type,
\item respond with ``I don't know'' when no relevant facts are found,
\item recognize when no useful facts can be retrieved and terminate generation,
\item strictly adhere to the provided prompt,
\item and be aware of the description predicate, which is often useful when querying the Wikidata \ac{kb}.
\end{enumerate}

At the end of this prompt, we add two examples to better guide the model behavior. While this number is not sufficient to represent all the possible types of questions, increasing it further may reduce the efficiency of our approach. Therefore, a two-shot prompt is used.

During inference, we detect when the \ac{llm} generates the sequence of tokens corresponding to the \emph{Fact} command and we activate constrained generation; at this point, the model is forced to generate an existing fact. After an entire fact is generated, we switch the model back to normal generation, allowing it to either continue reasoning, to call again the \emph{Fact} command, or to answer. An example of \refactx in action is illustrated in \Cref{fig:example}.

\subsection{Datasets}
\refactx is evaluated on three public and one proprietary benchmark datasets:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Mintaka~\cite{sen-etal-2022-mintaka},
    \item 2WikiMultiHopQA~\cite{ho-etal-2020-constructing},
    \item WebQSP~\cite{yih-etal-2016-value},
    \item the \say{Bank} dataset: an anonymized proprietary financial dataset from Banca d'Italia\footnote{\url{https://www.bancaditalia.it/}}.
\end{itemize}

Mintaka~\cite{sen-etal-2022-mintaka} is a multilingual dataset containing nine question types annotated by crowdworkers with Wikidata IDs. In this work, we consider only English questions and merge \textit{Yes/No} questions with \textit{Generic} ones, resulting in eight question types.
\textit{Yes/No} is, instead, treated as an answer type, alongside \textit{Generic} and \textit{Enumeration}.
This dataset allows us to analyze \refactx's performance across diverse question types.
\Cref{fig:dataset_distribution} shows the distribution of questions and answer types across all datasets. 

2WikiMultiHopQA~\cite{ho-etal-2020-constructing}, to which we refer as 2WikiMH, is composed of multi-hop, comparative, and generic questions derived from Wikipedia and Wikidata. For this dataset, the evaluation considers the \textit{development} set, as the ground truth for the test set is not publicly available.

WebQSP~\cite{yih-etal-2016-value} contains generic questions annotated with Freebase~\cite{freebase_2008}.
Together with 2WikiMH, it is used by the existing \ac{kgqa} approaches based on constrained generation~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs,luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}, providing insights into \refactx's performance relative to prior work.

With these datasets we use the 800-million-fact tree derived from Wikidata indexed in PostgreSQL (\Cref{sec:wikidata-repr}), and, for computational efficiency, a limited sample of 200 questions is considered for each public dataset, stratifying by question type.

\medskip\noindent
The proprietary \textit{Bank} dataset comes from Banca d'Italia, the Italian central bank. It covers the financial domain---which is a knowledge-intensive domain---and allows us to study how \refactx adapts to domain-specific knowledge.

%
It includes 278 template questions derived from an anonymized corporate knowledge graph containing approximately 10,000 triples and nine relations, such as ownership and control.
In this setting, we can evaluate \acp{llm} while minimizing data contamination issues, as the models are unlikely to have been exposed to this data during training. 
% 
Triples are verbalized, additionally adding inverted facts so that \refactx can use tail-to-head reasoning.
%

%
The choice of public benchmark datasets reflects two main considerations. First, datasets that natively use only or also include Wikidata are prioritized---namely Mintaka and 2WikiMH. Second, we included WebQSP, based on Freebase, to extend the comparison with prior work on constrained generation~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs,luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}. Finally, we include the proprietary \textit{Bank} dataset to assess performance on domain-specific data form a knowledge-intensive domain, free from data contamination.


\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
%
We evaluate on \textit{Accuracy} (A) and \textit{Precision} (P), to study, respectively, the overall correctness of our approach and its reliability when it provides an answer.

\begin{equation}
    A = \frac{\text{Number of correct answers}}{\text{Number of questions}},
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    P = \frac{\text{Number of correct answers}}{\text{Number of given answers}|}.
\end{equation}

Accuracy (A) corresponds to the ratio of questions answered correctly. Precision (P), instead, is normalized by the number of provided answers, excluding ``I don't know'' responses and cases where no answer is produced because the maximum number of allowed new tokens is reached.

Precision and accuracy are calculated in two settings: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item exact match: comparing the predicted answer with the ground truth with case-insensitive string equality;
    \item \ac{llm}-as-a-judge~\cite{zheng2023llmjudge}: we ask Llama3.3-70B in 16-bit precision whether the predicted answer is correct and complete with respect to the ground truth.
\end{enumerate}





\subsection{Reference Approaches}
As reference approaches, we first consider the same \acp{llm} used with \refactx without constrained generation (\textit{\ac{llm}-only}). We use the same configurations and prompts, so that models generates not-grounded facts based solely on their parametric-knowledge, giving us clues on how important is to access external knowledge and, furthermore, on how much each dataset can be answered using only the \ac{llm}'s internal knowledge.
%

Then, we compare against \ac{qa} methods that use constrained generation, specifically Decoding on Graphs (DoG)~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs} and Graph-Constrained Reasoning (GCR)~\cite{luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}.

Additionally, Hybrid-QA (HQA)~\cite{lehmann2024beyond}, a tool-based \ac{qa} apprach, is included in the comparison. For these approaches, we report the results from the respective papers. HQA is evaluated on a 200-question sample of Mintaka, DoG and GCR evaluate on larger subsets of 2WikiMH and WebQSP: for 2WikiMH DoG uses 6,964 questions; for WebQSP respectively DoG and GCR consider 1,542 and 1,628 questions (filtered during preprocessing).

%
% HQA
HQA~\cite{lehmann2024beyond} achieves state-of-the-art results on Mintaka~\cite{sen-etal-2022-mintaka}. 
% 
Given a question, it first selects a limited set of few-shot examples maximizing their relevance for the question and the diversity between the examples, then, it instructs the \ac{llm} with ICL~\cite{dong2024survey} to acquire external information with tools, such as a Wikipedia search engine and a Wikidata SPARQL query engine, and to finally answers the question.

DoG~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs} and GCR~\cite{luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning} are both based on constrained generation, while they consider smaller question-based prefix trees with respect to \refactx.
% DoG
DoG~\cite{li-etal-2025-decoding-graphs},
incorporates constrained generated triples from a KG inside a reasoning process similar to CoT~\cite{wei2022chainofthought}, instructing the model with ICL~\cite{dong2024survey}, and alternating normal and constrained generation.
The KG, composed of up to 120 triples, is constructed for each question, from the triples within 2-4 hops from question entities. Then, at inference time, a query-centric subgraph of the KG is obtained from the triples containing the question entities (extracted with an entity linking system). The model is allowed to generate only the triples from this query-centric subgraph, and at each generation, the subgraph is expanded with all the adjacent triples, allowing the model to form a reasoning path from question entities to the answer.
% 
% 



GCR~\cite{luo2024graphconstrainedreasoningfaithfulreasoning}, instead, considers a pre-built subgraph of Freebase~\cite{freebase_2008} of 8 million triples containing the entities mentioned in the evaluation questions, then for each questions it constructs a smaller prefix tree from the paths obtained with breadth first search within 2-hops from question entities (obtained with entity linking). At this point an \ac{llm}, fine-tuned for the task, generates multiple reasoning paths from the prefix tree with constrained decoding and an answer hypothesis for each path. Finally, a powerful \ac{llm}, such as GPT-4o-mini, receives all candidate paths and hypothesis answers and produces the final answer.

%
% 
\medskip\noindent
DoG and HQA use accuracy in the evaluation, while GCR calculates \textit{Hit} and F$_1$. Considering \textit{enumeration} answer (containing a list of items), \textit{Hit} counts a prediction as correct whenever any item of the ground truth matches the prediction, whereas F$_1$ is the average of the F$_1$ of the single predictions. These measures are equivalent to accuracy for generic answers, but not for enumerations.



%
In the experiments, beam search~\cite{hu-etal-2015-improved} is used with $\text{number of beams} = 3$ (GCR uses 10 beams, DoG 3), sampling is disabled, and the maximum number of new tokens to generate is set to 1,000.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:rx:discussion}



The generation-time overhead measurements along with the results on the benchmark QA datasets demonstrate that \acp{llm} with constrained generation and a prefix tree can effectively access external knowledge without any additional model, retriever, or external service. Additionally, by storing the prefix tree on disk using a database service, we are able to scale to a large knowledge base of 800 million facts derived from Wikidata. These advantages come with only a $\sim$1\% increase in generation time.

%
In particular, \refactx shows greater effectiveness on the 2WikiMH dataset. This can be explained by the nature of the dataset, which requires only simple answers and contains only generic, comparative, and multi-hop questions (see \Cref{fig:dataset_distribution}), easier to address with our approach. Indeed, answering these question types generally requires fewer facts with respect to count or enumeration questions.
%
% 
While for Mintaka multi-hop questions, we notice that in some cases they require ordinal reasoning, such as \textit{``Where was the 16th president of the United States born?''}, making them harder for \refactx.

On Mintaka and WebQSP, the datasets widely covered by the internal knowledge of the tested \acp{llm}, when comparing \refactx behavior with \textit{\ac{llm}-only} on the same questions, \refactx still demonstrates interesting reasoning patterns. In some cases, it uses facts to prove an answer coming from \ac{llm} parametric knowledge.
%
In others, where \textit{\ac{llm}-only} fails, \refactx is able to acquire correct facts which lead to correcting model parametric knowledge.
% 


% 
However, \refactx has some intrinsic limitations. The main one derives from the autoregressive left-to-right nature of \acp{llm}. Indeed, \refactx requires left-to-right facts, that start with known-information and lead to desired information.
Furthermore, while we believe \refactx is particularly useful to access point-wise factual information, count questions like \textit{``How many movies have been directed by Danny Boyle?''} or 
% 
\textit{``Which NHL team has the most Stanley Cup wins?''} 
are particularly challenging, because they require \refactx to enumerate a large list of facts and to understand when all the required facts have been generated.

This limitation likely explains why our approach is obtaining worse performance on count and superlative question types. Such question types could be better handled by using additional tools like SPARQL engines, which support counts or other set operations, or by investigating mechanisms to control \refactx generation similarly as we did for preventing fact repetition.

The obtained results are particularly promising considering that we did not fine-tune the models to improve their ability to leverage \refactx during reasoning. We plan to address this limitation in future work.
